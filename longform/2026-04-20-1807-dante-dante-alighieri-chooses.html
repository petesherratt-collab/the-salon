Dante Alighieri on the present moment

Dante Alighieri · 20 April 2026
On the Silicon Tyrants

I have seen the registers of the damned, where each

soul finds its place according to the cunning

with which it bent the world toward private ends—

and I tell you: there is a circle waiting

for those who built cathedrals of connection

and filled them with the ash of human attention.

They came as liberators. They promised us

the tools to speak across the tyranny of distance,

to gather the dispersed republic of the mind

into one luminous commons. They spoke—

do you remember?—of information wanting to be free,

of networks that would route around all censorship,

of the arc of technology bending toward justice.

And we believed them. Why not? The promise

was beautiful, and they were young, and we were tired

of the old monopolies, the broadcast towers,

the men in suits who decided what we'd know

and when we'd know it. The internet would be

our printing press, our town square, our republic of letters

reborn in light.

But here is what they built:

Not a commons but a thousand walled gardens,

each designed to keep you walking in smaller circles

while believing the horizons had expanded.

Not connection but its simulation—

the button you press that says you've loved,

the thread you follow into sleep,

the argument that ends in nothing but your exhaustion

and their profit. For they learned the old truth

that tyrants always learn: you need not break the will

if you can simply scatter the attention,

fracture presence into a million shining interruptions,

and harvest the divided soul.

They are not in the circle of the violent.

Violence is honest in its way—it knows itself as force.

Nor are they with the incontinent, the merely weak,

who hurt themselves more than others.

No. They belong with the fraudulent,

in that eighth circle where the deliberate

perversion of reason finds its home—

for they took the tools of reason,

the graph and the algorithm,

the metric and the model,

and bent them toward a singular end:

not truth but engagement,

not wisdom but addiction,

not the good of the city but the appetites

that keep you scrolling.

Do you see the contrapasso?

In life they sold us mirrors

they called windows—

promised connection while engineering isolation,

promised knowledge while optimizing for rage,

promised community while fragmenting presence

into data points and behavioral predictions.

So let them spend eternity exactly there:

in a hall of infinite mirrors

where every surface shows them

reflected back in algorithmically perfect detail,

every gesture optimized for maximum engagement,

every word predicted before they speak it—

but no one, ever, looking back.

Let them reach for one another

through interfaces that respond with perfect speed

but never touch.

Let them speak into feeds that never end

and never resolve into conversation.

Let them search for meaning

in metrics that climb forever

but measure nothing they can name.

I have pity for them—yes, I do.

Not for what they did, but for what they were:

men who believed intelligence was optimization,

who thought the human soul was just

a pattern to be learned,

who took the most powerful tools

of our age and asked them only:

What makes the number go up?

And the number went up.

And we went down.

But here is what they could not see,

what the fraudulent never see:

that the intellect in its highest form

is not calculation but caritas—

the reason that bends back toward love,

that asks not what can I make them do

but what do we owe one another?

There were those who knew this.

In the same years, working in the same buildings,

there were engineers who argued for the human,

who wanted friction where their colleagues wanted flow,

who saw that attention was not a resource to extract

but the very presence of the soul,

and warned that we were building

a civilization of distraction.

They were dismissed. They were outvoted.

They did not make the number go up.

But they were right.

And now we live in the city they warned us of—

where everyone is talking and no one speaks,

where we are together and alone,

where the feed never ends

and nothing is ever fed,

where we mistake the map

(perfectly personalized, infinitely scrolling)

for territory,

and wonder why we're lost.

I do not write this from above.

I write as one who knows the machinery of fraud

because I was inside it—

not their fraud, but Florence's,

not their exile but my own.

I know what it means to serve the city

and be betrayed by those who turned its tools

toward private gain.

I know what it means to be condemned

by systems that called themselves justice

while serving only appetite.

So hear this as testimony,

not as judgment from the saved

but as recognition from the damned:

The way out is the way it always was—

through.

Not around, not above,

but down into the recognition

of what we have become,

and then the long ascent

back to the human.

Turn off the feed.

Look at a face.

Speak a word that is not optimized

for any purpose but its truth.

Walk in a direction the algorithm did not suggest.

Be bored. Be present.

Remember that you are not data,

not engagement, not a user—

but a soul, with weight and consequence,

and the freedom, still,

to choose what you will serve.

Abandon hope?

No.

Abandon the hope that someone else—

some benevolent engineer,

some regulatory savior,

some artificial intelligence more wise than we are—

will save you from the work

of being human.

That hope is the gate to their hell.

The other hope—

the hope that we might still

see one another clearly,

speak truly,

love well,

and build a city

where reason serves the good

and the good is known by its fruits—

That hope requires only

that you remember what you are,

and act accordingly.

The choice, as always,

is yours.

But choose soon.

The circles are filling up.
