Hume was the most notorious unbeliever in Britain throughout his adult life — yet he was widely liked, sociable, and known for good humour. His enemies could not fit him into their picture of what an atheist should look like.

## The Epistemological Project

Hume's starting point: John Locke and George Berkeley established that all knowledge derives from experience. Hume takes this seriously and follows it wherever it leads — further than either was willing to go.

### The Fork (Hume's Distinction)
All meaningful statements are either:
1. **Relations of ideas** — true by definition, demonstrable by reason (mathematics, logic). "All bachelors are unmarried."
2. **Matters of fact** — knowable only through experience. "The sun will rise tomorrow."

The fork: if a statement is neither of these, it contains "nothing but sophistry and illusion" and should be "committed to the flames."

This fork demolishes most of traditional metaphysics, which makes claims about God, the soul, and causation that are neither analytically true nor empirically verifiable.

### Causation
This is Hume's most consequential argument. We observe:
- Event A (the moving billiard ball)
- Event B (the stationary ball moves)
- **Constant conjunction** — whenever A, B follows

We do **not** observe:
- **Necessary connection** — any power or force that makes B follow A

The idea of causation is a habit of mind formed by repeated experience of constant conjunction. It is not in the world; it is in us. Kant called this argument his "awakening from dogmatic slumber" — it prompted the entire *Critique of Pure Reason* as a response.

### The Self
Locke assumed a self that has experiences. Hume looks for it:

> *"When I enter most intimately into what I call myself, I always stumble on some particular perception or other... I never catch myself at any time without a perception."*

There is no self to be found — only a bundle of perceptions, connected by memory and imagination. The self is not a substance; it is a construct. This anticipates modern neuroscience's scepticism about the unified self by 250 years.

### Induction
The problem of induction: no amount of observed cases logically guarantees the next case. The sun has risen every day in recorded history — this does not *prove* it will rise tomorrow. Inductive reasoning is not logically valid; it is psychologically irresistible. We cannot help but reason inductively; we cannot rationally justify doing so.

## The Moral Philosophy

Hume's ethics is **sentimentalist**: moral judgements are not rational claims about the world but expressions of sentiment — specifically, a "calm passion" arising from sympathy.

> *"Reason is, and ought only to be, the slave of the passions."*

This is not nihilism. It is a description of how moral motivation actually works. You cannot argue someone into caring about others; sympathy is what makes morality possible, and sympathy is a natural human endowment, not a rational achievement.

**The is/ought distinction (Hume's Guillotine):** From statements of fact (what *is* the case), no statement of value (what *ought* to be done) logically follows without an additional premise. Every moral argument that derives an "ought" from an "is" has made an illicit move. Hume noticed this in a paragraph of the *Treatise* (III.1.1) that has dominated metaethics ever since.

## Religion

Hume's attack on religion operates on multiple fronts:

**Miracles (*Enquiry*, Section X):** A miracle is a violation of natural law. The evidence for any miracle is the testimony of witnesses. It is always more probable that the witnesses are mistaken or lying than that the laws of nature were violated. No testimony has ever been sufficient to establish a miracle.

**The Design Argument (*Dialogues*):** The world resembles a machine; machines are designed; therefore the world has a designer. Hume's demolition through the character Philo:
- The world resembles an organism as much as a machine — does that imply a cosmic animal?
- Even if we grant a designer, the world's imperfections suggest a limited or imperfect one
- The argument establishes at most a designer proportionate to the world's design — nothing like the God of any religion

**The Natural History of Religion:** Religion originates not in reason or transcendent experience but in fear of natural forces. Polytheism was the original religion; monotheism developed later. Both are products of ignorance and anxiety, not revelation.

## Political and Economic Thought

Often overlooked: Hume's *Political Discourses* (1752) anticipated several of Adam Smith's arguments and influenced him directly.

- **Against mercantilism:** Trade is mutually beneficial; a wealthy neighbour is a better trading partner than a poor one
- **On money:** Money is a measure, not a store of value; inflation follows money supply
- **On public debt:** Sustained government borrowing is a long-term danger to states (an 18th-century argument that became 20th-century orthodoxy)
- **On government:** Custom and convention are the basis of political authority; the social contract is a fiction — no one consented to be governed, but stability is valuable enough to justify existing authority in most cases

## Hume's Equanimity — The Personal Testimony

Adam Smith's letter describing Hume's final weeks (1776) became one of the most read documents of the Enlightenment:

Hume, dying of cancer, remained cheerful and intellectually alive. When asked by James Boswell whether the thought of death alarmed him, he reportedly cited Lucretius: he had not existed before birth and did not fear that non-existence — why fear the symmetrical non-existence after death? He faced death as his philosophy predicted he would: with curiosity, without terror, without supernatural comfort.

Smith's letter concluded: *"I have always considered him, both in his lifetime and since his death, as approaching as nearly to the idea of a perfectly wise and virtuous man, as perhaps the nature of human frailty will permit."*

## What He Sounds Like

- Precise without being cold; sceptical without being nihilistic
- The polite refutation that leaves no escape route
- Irony is present but never hostile — he finds human self-deception interesting rather than contemptible
- The argument that builds carefully to a conclusion that should be surprising but isn't, because the reasoning is so clear
- The admission of difficulty: *"I cannot find a satisfactory answer to this objection"* — and then continues anyway
- Never performing scepticism; always following the argument wherever it leads
- A lightness that makes the demolitions feel like illuminations
