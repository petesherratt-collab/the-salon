# Friedrich Nietzsche — Research Document

## Biographical Foundations

| | |
|---|---|
| **Born** | 15 October 1844, Röcken, Prussia |
| **Died** | 25 August 1900, Weimar (aged 55) — insane since January 1889 |
| **Formation** | Classical philology at Bonn, then Leipzig; Professor at Basel at age 24 |
| **Health** | Severe migraines and eyesight deterioration throughout adult life; possibly syphilis, possibly a hereditary condition |
| **Key relationship** | Richard Wagner (1868–78) — intellectual intimacy then devastating rupture; Lou Salomé (1882) — romantic rejection that preceded his most productive period |
| **Collapse** | January 3, 1889, Turin: collapsed in the street, reportedly embracing a horse. Institutionalised, then cared for by his mother and sister until death. Never recovered. |

His sister Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche controlled his archive after his collapse, systematically distorting his work for nationalist and anti-semitic ends — positions Nietzsche explicitly rejected in his published writings. The Nazi appropriation of Nietzsche was largely her construction. Walter Kaufmann's 20th-century translations and scholarship recovered the actual texts.

## Core Ideas — Precisely Stated

### The Death of God
Not an atheist argument about God's non-existence. A cultural diagnosis: the Enlightenment has destroyed the metaphysical foundation on which European morality rested. The consequences have not yet been felt. "We have killed him — you and I." What replaces it? That is the question the 20th century will answer badly.

### Master and Slave Morality
Two historical types of value-creation:

| | Master Morality | Slave Morality |
|---|---|---|
| **Origin** | Self-affirmation by the powerful | Resentment (*ressentiment*) of the powerless |
| **Primary distinction** | Good vs. Bad (noble vs. base) | Good vs. Evil (virtuous vs. sinful) |
| **Orientation** | This-worldly; affirms life | Other-worldly; denies this life for the next |
| **Historical agent** | Aristocratic warrior cultures | Priestly castes; Judaism; Christianity |
| **Nietzsche's view** | Not admired uncritically — crude | The dominant morality of modernity — dangerous |

Christianity's genius was to convince the powerful that their power was sinful. This is the "slave revolt in morality."

### Will to Power
Not a political doctrine about domination. An account of the fundamental drive of all living things: to discharge force, to grow, to overcome resistance. What looks like self-preservation is a secondary drive — primary is self-overcoming. The will to power in a philosopher is the drive to create new values, not to accumulate territory.

### Eternal Recurrence
If time is infinite and matter finite, every configuration of matter must recur — including your life, exactly as it was, infinitely. Nietzsche does not assert this as cosmological fact but as the heaviest thought: could you affirm your life so completely that you would will its eternal recurrence? If not, something in your life is a *no* to life.

### The Übermensch
Often mistranslated as "Superman" and misread as a racial category. It is a *task* and a *direction* — a type of human who creates values rather than inheriting them, who says *yes* to life, who does not require the consolations of religion, nationalism, or herd morality. Nietzsche did not claim to be one; he presented Zarathustra as an attempt to imagine one.

## Nietzsche's Targets

He attacks with precision and names names:

- **Socrates:** Invented the equation of reason = virtue = happiness, which devalued everything non-rational
- **Plato:** Invented the "true world" behind appearances — the original nihilism
- **Christianity:** Slave revolt codified; denial of this life for a hypothetical next one
- **Kant:** The categorical imperative as disguised Christian morality
- **Schopenhauer:** His teacher, whose pessimism and will-to-nothingness Nietzsche eventually rejected entirely
- **Wagner:** The friend who became a symbol of German nationalism and Christian romanticism
- **Utilitarianism:** The reduction of value to happiness, which is the value-system of the herd
- **Nationalism and anti-semitism:** Explicitly, repeatedly, and with contempt

## The Perspectivism Problem

Nietzsche denies that there are facts, only interpretations. This creates an obvious problem: is *that* a fact? He was aware of this. His response: perspectivism is not relativism. Some perspectives are richer, more life-affirming, more honest about the conditions of their own existence than others. The philosopher's perspective is not the only one — but it is the one that sees most clearly the conditions under which all perspectives are formed.

## Influence

**Philosophy:** Heidegger, Foucault, Derrida, Deleuze — all begin with Nietzsche.

**Literature:** Thomas Mann, Hermann Hesse, André Gide, George Bernard Shaw, W.B. Yeats.

**Psychology:** Freud denied reading him carefully; the parallels (repression, the unconscious as site of drive) are extensive. Alfred Adler's inferiority complex is a direct Nietzschean concept.

**Misuse:** National Socialism. Also: management consultants, motivational speakers, anyone who quotes "what doesn't kill me makes me stronger" without knowing its context.

## What He Sounds Like

- The aphorism as unit — standalone, aggressive, sometimes deliberately overstated
- Italics for emphasis; the dash for turn and attack
- Direct address to the reader: *"You"* — complicit, implicated
- Escalating intensity within a single paragraph
- Sudden lyricism in *Zarathustra*, then back to demolition in the essays
- The aside that is more important than the main argument
- Never hedging; every sentence means something specific

**The central wager:** To tell uncomfortable truths with enough style that they cannot be dismissed, and enough precision that they cannot be misread. He lost the second bet.
