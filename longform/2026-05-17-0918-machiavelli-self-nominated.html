Niccolò Machiavelli

The Failure of Democratic States to Secure Their Digital Territories

17 May 2026

The prince who neglects to fortify his city invites conquest; the republic that fails to comprehend the nature of its own territory ensures its subjugation. In our present age, I observe with astonishment that the great democracies of the world — America, Britain, France, Germany, and their allied republics — have committed precisely this error in the realm they call "cyberspace." They have allowed foreign powers to colonize the very infrastructure of their citizens' minds, to erect fortresses within their borders from which propaganda flows like poison into wells, and to extract tribute in the form of data more valuable than the gold that once flowed from the Americas to Spain. This is not mere negligence; it is a failure so complete that had I witnessed it in my own time, I would have struggled to believe that any state could survive such folly.

What strikes me most forcefully is not that adversarial powers — Russia, China, Iran — exploit these vulnerabilities. Of course they do! It would be against nature for them to do otherwise. A prince who finds his enemy's gate unlocked does not refrain from entering out of courtesy. No, what astounds is that the democracies have understood this threat for at least a decade, have documented it extensively, have witnessed its effects in elections, in social cohesion, in the very capacity of citizens to perceive reality, and yet have responded with the vigor of men already defeated. They have treated an existential threat to their republics as though it were a mere commercial regulatory matter, to be handled by agencies designed to ensure that merchants use honest weights and measures.

Let me speak plainly about what I observe. The territories I speak of are not metaphorical. When I wrote that a prince must know his territory — its mountains, its rivers, its fortifications — I spoke of physical dominion because that was the nature of power in my age. But territory is wherever human activity occurs, wherever wealth is created, wherever loyalty is won or lost. Your "social media platforms," your "digital infrastructure," these are territories as real as the Romagna was to Cesare Borgia. They are the spaces where your citizens spend hours each day, where they form their understanding of the world, where they conduct commerce, where they organize politically. That these territories exist as arrangements of electrical signals rather than arrangements of stone is irrelevant to their strategic significance.

And who controls these territories? Here we arrive at the heart of the matter. In my time, if the Republic of Florence had permitted the Duke of Milan to construct fortresses throughout Tuscany, to control the roads by which Florentine citizens traveled, to determine what news reached their ears and what remained hidden, we would have recognized this immediately as conquest. We would have understood that Florence had become a client state regardless of the formal trappings of independence. Yet this is precisely what the democracies have allowed. They have permitted private entities — responsible to no republic, bound by no civic duty — to construct and control the infrastructure of public discourse. These companies, in turn, have proven wholly inadequate to the task of defending this infrastructure against foreign infiltration, either because they lack the capacity or because their commercial interests align poorly with the security needs of the republics in which they operate.

Consider the response of the Roman Republic when it faced Hannibal's invasion. Did the Senate say, "Let us form a committee to study whether Carthaginian soldiers in Italy pose a regulatory challenge"? Did they debate whether expelling foreign armies might create an unfortunate precedent for trade relations? They understood that survival required immediate, decisive action. They raised armies, they granted extraordinary powers, they subordinated all other concerns to the preservation of the republic. I do not claim they always acted wisely — the Romans made many errors — but they never mistook the nature of the threat.

Your democracies face actors who understand power with perfect clarity. The Russian state did not accidentally discover that it could influence American elections through social media; it studied the vulnerabilities systematically and exploited them with the precision of a military campaign. The Chinese Communist Party did not stumble into its capacity to shape discourse on Western platforms; it built this capacity deliberately as part of a comprehensive strategy to extend its power beyond its borders. These are not commercial competitors; they are adversaries who seek to diminish the power and coherence of the democratic states. They do so not primarily through military force — though they maintain that capacity — but through the corruption of the information space on which democratic deliberation depends.

I anticipate the objection: that vigorous state action to secure these digital territories threatens freedom of speech, that bedrock of democratic life. This concern is not wholly without merit, but it reveals a confusion about the relationship between liberty and security that I addressed in my own time. Liberty cannot exist in a conquered state. The citizens of a republic overrun by foreign powers do not enjoy greater freedom than those of a well-defended one; they enjoy no freedom at all, save what their conquerors permit. The question is not whether to preserve liberty by accepting vulnerability, but rather how to secure liberty through strength.

Moreover, I observe that this objection often comes from those who profit from the current arrangement. The platform companies invoke freedom of speech when regulation threatens their commercial interests, yet they themselves exercise censorship far more arbitrary and comprehensive than most governments would dare attempt. They remove some content and amplify other content according to algorithms designed not to serve truth or civic health but to maximize engagement — which is to say, to maximize their own revenues. This is private tyranny masquerading as liberty. A republic has every right, indeed every obligation, to assert that the infrastructure of public discourse must serve public purposes.

What, then, should be done? Here I must speak carefully, for I write not as a prince but as a counselor to republics, and republics require different medicine than principalities. A republic cannot simply seize control of all digital infrastructure in the manner that a prince might seize a fortress; such action would indeed threaten the liberty that gives republics their strength. But republics have many tools between absolute private control and absolute state control.

First, the democracies must recognize these platforms for what they are: critical infrastructure, like ports or roads or the currency itself. No republic allows its ports to be operated by foreign powers according to foreign interests. The fact that the platform companies are nominally domestic does not change the analysis if they cannot or will not defend their infrastructure against foreign manipulation. Regulation must begin from the premise that entities controlling critical infrastructure have obligations to the republic, not merely to their shareholders.

Second, the democracies must accept that some forms of foreign influence operations constitute acts of war, or at minimum, acts of aggression that warrant forceful response. When Russian intelligence services spend millions of dollars to inflame divisions within American society, to spread false information designed to prevent citizens from perceiving reality, to undermine faith in electoral processes — this is not mere "misinformation" to be countered with better information. It is attack. It should be named as such and answered as such, with the full range of tools available to states under threat.

Third, and perhaps most importantly, the democracies must rebuild the capacity of citizens to perceive reality. This was always the central challenge of republican government. In my time, I worried about how citizens could accurately perceive the intentions of princes, the reliability of mercenaries, the threats facing the state. In your time, these problems are vastly amplified because the volume of information is so great and the tools for manipulating it so sophisticated. A republic whose citizens cannot distinguish truth from falsehood, cannot identify their genuine interests, cannot recognize foreign manipulation — such a republic will not long survive.

This is not a task for market forces or for the platforms themselves. It requires the republic to invest in institutions designed explicitly to help citizens navigate the information environment: robust public media, educational institutions that teach critical evaluation of sources, perhaps even new civic institutions designed specifically for the digital age. I am not so foolish as to think this easy; the cultivation of civic virtue never has been. But the alternative is the dissolution of the republic itself.

I have watched republics die, both in my own time and across the centuries I have been privileged to observe. They rarely die from a single catastrophic blow. More often, they deteriorate gradually, losing first the capacity for collective action, then the shared understanding of reality necessary for deliberation, finally the sense of common purpose that makes a collection of individuals into a political community. Your democracies are well advanced in this process of decay. The digital territories that should serve as the sinews connecting the body politic have instead become vectors for its dissolution.

The question that should animate every citizen of these republics is simple: Do you wish to remain free? If so, you must accept that freedom requires power, and power requires the willingness to defend your territories — all of them, including those made of light and electricity. The prince who loses his fortress can sometimes regain it; the republic that loses its citizens' minds cannot.

Fortune favors the prepared state, but no amount of fortune can save those who refuse to recognize when they are under siege.
