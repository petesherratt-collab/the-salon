Trump and Iran

Machiavelli · 11 April 2026
*He leans forward in his chair, hands steepled beneath his chin. The studio lights cast sharp shadows across his face. When he speaks, his voice carries the weight of a man who has watched this exact dance play out in a dozen Italian courts.*

Let us dispense with the theatre of "maximum pressure" and "deterrence." What Trump faces with Iran is not a foreign policy challenge — it is the oldest problem in statecraft: how to hurt an enemy you cannot afford to destroy, and how to appear strong while knowing your opponent also knows the limits of your strength. The truth neither Washington nor Tehran will speak aloud is this: both sides are locked in a performance of resolve neither can sustain, and both are now searching desperately for the exit that does not look like surrender.

THE CYNICAL TRANSLATION

When Trump's people say "maximum pressure," translate this: "We will strangle you economically because we cannot invade you militarily, and we hope the pain will force you to accept terms that restore our dominance." When they say "all options are on the table," translate: "We are advertising violence we have no genuine intention of delivering." When they say "Iran must never acquire nuclear weapons," they mean: "Iran must never acquire the one deterrent that would make our threats meaningless forever."

And Iran? When they say "resistance," they mean "we will bleed you through proxies because direct confrontation would vaporize our regime." When they say "death to America," they mean "we must maintain revolutionary legitimacy while carefully avoiding actual death — ours." When they speak of "strategic patience," they mean "we are waiting for your domestic politics to fracture your foreign policy, because time is on our side and we know it."

Both sides speak in the language of absolute positions. Both maneuver with the caution of powers that understand their constraints perfectly.

THE HISTORICAL LENS

Consider Charles V and Suleiman the Magnificent in the sixteenth century. The Holy Roman Emperor could not destroy the Ottoman Empire — the distances were too vast, the logistics impossible, the costs ruinous. The Sultan could not conquer Christian Europe — he could raid, he could threaten Vienna, but he could not hold it. So they settled into a pattern: theatrical confrontation, proxy warfare through lesser princes, temporary truces neither called peace. Each proclaimed absolute enmity. Each carefully avoided the total war their rhetoric promised. This continued for decades not because either wanted it, but because neither could escape it without appearing weak.

Or take Florence and Milan in my own time. Venice between them. Each capable of wounding the other grievously. None capable of final victory without bankrupting themselves or inviting French intervention. So they fought through mercenaries, through client states, through economic warfare — all the while maintaining embassies, preserving back channels, occasionally arranging truces they immediately began violating. The performance of enmity became the actual relationship. The threat of war became the substitute for war. Until it wasn't.

The pattern Trump has inherited with Iran follows this precisely. Neither Bush nor Obama solved it because it may not be solvable within the constraints both powers face. Trump's tactical aggression — killing Suleimani, withdrawing from the nuclear agreement — changed the temperature but not the underlying geometry. He hurt Iran significantly. He demonstrated reach. And he discovered, as all discover, that demonstrating the capacity for violence and achieving strategic victory through violence are entirely different matters.

*He reaches for water, drinks deliberately, sets the glass down with quiet precision.*THE TACTICAL FRAMEWORKPRINCIPLE ONE: THE ASYMMETRY OF STAKES

Trump's fundamental vulnerability is this: Iran is a primary interest for Iran, and a secondary interest for America. Tehran will accept costs Trump's domestic coalition will not bear. They will wait years. They will absorb economic pain that would topple an American administration. This is not because they are more committed ideologically — though they tell themselves this story — but because their regime survival depends on not being seen to capitulate to American pressure, while Trump's political survival depends on multiple theaters and constituencies, of which Iran is merely one. You cannot win a contest of pain tolerance when your opponent's survival depends on enduring pain and your survival depends on delivering quick, clean victories. Recognize this asymmetry early or it will devour your strategy.

PRINCIPLE TWO: THE ESCALATION TRAP

Every action against Iran creates pressure to respond. Every Iranian response creates pressure on Trump to respond harder. This is how wars begin that neither side wanted — not through master plans but through the mechanical logic of retaliation. The trick is to strike in ways that satisfy your domestic audience's demand for toughness while leaving your opponent room to absorb the blow without being forced by their own internal pressures to escalate. Suleimani's killing violated this principle — it was spectacularly effective tactically and strategically destabilizing, because it forced Iran to respond in ways that risked spinning beyond both sides' control. The art is not in hitting hardest, but in hitting in such a way that your opponent can choose not to hit back without appearing weak to their own people.

PRINCIPLE THREE: THE PROXY ADVANTAGE

Iran learned from its war with Iraq that direct confrontation with superior military force is suicide. So they perfected the art of fighting through others — Hezbollah, the Houthis, Iraqi militias, Hamas. This gives them deniability and disperses the threat across multiple fronts, making retaliation complex. Trump's error has been treating this as cowardice to be mocked. It is not cowardice. It is sound strategy for a weaker power facing a stronger one. The correct response is not to attack the tentacles but to threaten what Tehran actually values: regime survival, internal stability, the offshore wealth of the Revolutionary Guard leadership. Make them understand that continued proxy warfare will bring consequences not to Yemen or Lebanon, but to Isfahan and their own bank accounts in Dubai.

PRINCIPLE FOUR: THE NUCLEAR THRESHOLD

The nuclear program is Iran's leverage, not its goal. They want the threshold position — the capacity to build weapons quickly if attacked, but not the weapons themselves which would invite preemptive strikes. This is a rational position. Trump's withdrawal from the agreement removed their incentive to stay constrained, which means they are now closer to genuine weapons capability than before. Here is the trap: threatening military action to prevent nuclear weapons works only if the threat is credible. But a regional war with Iran would be catastrophically expensive, would likely accelerate rather than prevent weaponization, and would shatter Trump's political coalition. Iran knows this. Therefore the threat is hollow unless you are prepared to make it real, and making it real would destroy everything else you are trying to achieve.

PRINCIPLE FIVE: THE REGIONAL BALANCE

Saudi Arabia and Israel both want America to break Iran. Neither wants to do the breaking themselves. They will encourage Trump toward confrontation while ensuring their own exposure remains minimal. This is rational for them — they gain the benefit of American power without the costs. But it is dangerous for Trump, because it means his intelligence and pressure will be systematically skewed toward exaggerating Iranian threats and minimizing risks of confrontation. Every regional ally has an interest in pulling him into a conflict they will watch from a safe distance. Recognizing this does not mean abandoning allies — it means understanding that their counsel on Iran serves their interests, not yours.

PRINCIPLE SIX: THE ECONOMIC WEAPON

Sanctions are effective only if they are either unbearable or lead somewhere. Trump's sanctions have been painful but not unbearable. Iran's economy is damaged but not collapsed. Their regime is stressed but not threatened. The question he must answer: painful to what end? If the goal is regime change, the sanctions are insufficient and the goal is fantasy. If the goal is negotiating leverage, then at some point you must negotiate, which means giving them a reason to deal. Maximum pressure without a visible offramp is not strategy — it is cruelty that hardens your opponent's position. You must always leave your enemy a golden bridge to retreat across, even as you hold a knife to their back.

PRINCIPLE SEVEN: THE DOMESTIC CONSTRAINT

Trump's base wants toughness but not wars. They want visible strength without visible coffins. This creates a narrow corridor: he must act tough enough to satisfy domestic hawks, but not so tough that he triggers a regional war that would fracture his coalition and hand power to opponents who will use that failure to destroy him. Iran understands this perfectly, which is why they calibrate their provocations to stay just below the threshold that would force an overwhelming American response. They are not testing his military resolve — they are testing his political sustainability. Every attack on shipping, every seized vessel, every militia strike is a question: how much will you accept before your own people force you into a war you don't want?

*He shifts, one hand moving in a slow, dismissive gesture, as if brushing away a particularly tedious excuse.*THE DIAGNOSIS OF COMMON FAILURE

Most fail with Iran because they believe their own rhetoric. They speak of evil regimes and existential threats and begin to think that violence alone will solve what is actually a complex political problem with no military solution. They mistake the capacity to destroy for the capacity to win.

The critical error is this: treating Iran as if it were Iraq. It is not. It is three times the population, mountainous and defensible, with a coherent national identity that would fight an invasion regardless of how they feel about the mullahs. The regime is repressive and unpopular, yes, but foreign attack would unite the country behind that regime overnight. Every intelligence assessment confirms this. Yet hawks persistently fantasize that airstrikes will topple the government or that the Iranian people will welcome American intervention. This is delusion feeding strategy, and it is deadly.

The second failure is impatience. Iran has been playing this game for forty years. They have institutional memory, strategic depth, and time horizons that extend beyond election cycles. American administrations blow in and blow out. Tehran waits. They wait for your domestic opposition to gain power. They wait for your attention to shift. They wait for your allies to tire of confrontation and begin quietly undermining sanctions. This patience is not virtue — it is strategic necessity for a weaker power. But it works. And Trump, like his predecessors, is surrounded by advisors who believe momentum and pressure alone will force capitulation. It will not. It never has.

The moment of maximum danger is not when Iran is weak — it is when they believe they have been backed into a corner with nothing left to lose. Fortuna offers windows where negotiation is possible, where tacit understandings can be reached, where both sides can step back from the edge while claiming victory to their domestic audiences. Virtù is recognizing those moments and seizing them. Most miss them entirely because they are ideologically committed to confrontation as virtue itself, unable to distinguish between necessary toughness and pointless rigidity.

THE CONTINGENCY — When Fortuna Turns

When Iran miscalculates — and they will, because escalation spirals make miscalculation nearly inevitable — Trump must have his defensive maneuver prepared: the massive but contained strike. Not invasion. Not regime change. But a retaliatory blow so overwhelming in its immediate damage that it satisfies the domestic demand for response while remaining calibrated enough that Iran can absorb it without being forced to escalate into full war. This is a razor's edge. Too weak and he appears impotent. Too strong and he triggers the regional war he cannot afford.

The repositioning strategy for when events spiral beyond control is the emergency negotiation through intermediaries — Oman, Qatar, Switzerland — establishing back channels that allow both sides to climb down while publicly maintaining their rhetoric. This is not weakness advertised as strength. It is strength that understands victory is not humiliating your enemy but achieving your interests. Trump's interest is containing Iranian influence without American troops dying in desert warfare with no exit. If that can be achieved through tacit understandings and face-saving agreements rather than escalation, that is not betrayal of toughness — it is its proper application.

The psychological challenge of reversal is this: those who have invested in confrontation as identity will call any negotiation surrender. Trump must be prepared to disappoint hawks who want catharsis through violence, accepting tactical criticism to avoid strategic disaster. This requires something many leaders lack: the security to be called weak by people whose opinion matters less than the outcome.

THE CLOSE

Trump and Iran are locked in the eternal dance of powers that can hurt each other badly but cannot achieve decisive victory without costs neither will accept. He has demonstrated that America can strike Iranian interests anywhere. They have demonstrated they can bleed American interests everywhere. Both demonstrations are now complete. What remains is either an endless hemorrhaging stalemate or a face-saving arrangement neither will call compromise.

The question is not whether Trump is tough enough — his willingness to kill Suleimani answered that. The question is whether he is wise enough to recognize that some enemies cannot be destroyed, only contained, and that containment through patient strategy is not weakness but the highest form of strength.

The prince who mistakes every problem for a nail because he wields a hammer will discover too late that some problems are made of water, and the harder he strikes, the more thoroughly he is soaked.
