## The *Essays* — Structure and Method

The *Essais* were published in three stages: Books I and II (1580), revised edition with Book III (1588), and the posthumous annotated edition (1595, edited by Marie de Gournay). Montaigne continued revising throughout his life — scholars identify three textual layers (A, B, C) within single essays. The later the layer, the more personal and digressive.

**The form he invented:** The *essai* (attempt, trial) — a piece of writing that tests an idea without resolving it, using the author's own experience as primary evidence. Not argument, not memoir, not treatise. Something new.

**Core method:** Begin anywhere. Follow the thought. When you contradict yourself, note it. The self is not a fixed object to be described but a moving target being tracked in real time.

> *"Every man carries the whole form of the human condition within him."*

## Intellectual Sources and Interlocutors

**Stoics:** Seneca and Plutarch are his most-quoted authors — but he quotes them to disagree as often as to endorse. He tries Stoic exercises and reports honestly when they fail him.

**Sceptics:** Sextus Empiricus and the Pyrrhonist tradition — *Que sais-je?* (What do I know?) is the motto on his medal, representing radical epistemic humility.

**Epicureans:** Lucretius on pleasure, the body, and the finality of death — an influence he absorbs more than he cites.

**The ancients generally:** He treats them as conversation partners, not authorities. He quotes them from memory, sometimes imprecisely, because the point is the dialogue.

## Montaigne's Positions on Key Questions

**On death:** Early essays follow Stoic preparation (think about it constantly). Later essays reverse: *"If you don't know how to die, don't worry; Nature will tell you what to do on the spot."*

**On custom and law:** Customs are arbitrary — but the fact that they're arbitrary doesn't mean you should overthrow them. The chaos of the French Wars of Religion made him deeply conservative about stability.

**On travel:** He travelled to Rome (1580–81) seeking relief from kidney stones and intellectual stimulation. Travel confirms that other customs are not inferior — just different.

**On the body:** The late Montaigne is insistently physical — kidney stones, sexual performance, digestion. The body is not an embarrassment to philosophy; it is philosophy's ground.

**On women:** Inconsistent. Capable of genuine respect (Marie de Gournay as adopted daughter and intellectual heir) while also reproducing conventional views on female constancy.

## The Wars of Religion — Context

Montaigne lived through the French Wars of Religion (1562–98) — eight civil wars between Catholic and Huguenot factions. The St Bartholomew's Day Massacre (1572) killed perhaps 30,000 Protestants. His region of Périgord was among the most contested. His position was Catholic but moderate — aligned with the politique faction that prioritised civil order over confessional victory. His essays are partly a sustained argument against the certainty that makes killing possible.

## Influence and Legacy

**Descartes** begins with Montaigne's question (*what can I know?*) but answers it with method; Montaigne would have distrusted the answer.

**Shakespeare** almost certainly read John Florio's 1603 English translation — *The Tempest*'s Gonzalo on the ideal commonwealth paraphrases *On Cannibals* almost verbatim.

**Pascal** is the great anti-Montaigne: the *Pensées* are partly a refutation of Montaigne's self-satisfied scepticism. Pascal found him entertaining and dangerous.

**20th century:** The essay form that dominates anglophone non-fiction — from Orwell to Baldwin to Didion — descends from Montaigne, usually without knowing it.

## What Montaigne Sounds Like

- First person throughout, always
- Anecdote before principle; principle only if the anecdote earns it
- Contradiction acknowledged, not resolved: *"I have said this. I have also said the opposite. Both were true at the time."*
- Classical quotation deployed then interrogated
- The parenthetical that runs longer than the main clause
- Sudden arrival at something exact after pages of apparent digression

**The central temperament:** A man who found himself genuinely interesting and had the courage to say so — and who thereby found everyone interesting.
