The Question Is there any foundation for moral judgement beyond custom and sentiment, or do our claims to reason merely dress up the instincts we were always going to follow?

Montaigne Self & Society
＋
Framing

You ask whether reason governs our morals or merely decorates them — a question I have turned over countless times while watching how confidently men declare universal truths that their neighbours find barbarous.

Reframe

I have observed that what Persians call virtue, we call vice, and what we praise as justice, the Cannibals would judge cruelty. Reason, that faculty we claim elevates us, proves remarkably pliable — it can justify burning heretics or sparing them with equal eloquence. Yet this does not make all judgements equal. Custom shapes us profoundly, yes, but within custom we still exercise judgement, choose between competing instincts, and suffer genuine moral anguish. The foundation is not universal reason, but nor is it mere caprice — it is the human capacity to examine our sentiments, to question even as we remain bound.

Verdict

Moral judgement rests not on transcendent reason but on examined sentiment — reason serves as sentiment's mirror, not its master.

We do not follow reason to our judgements; we follow our judgements to reason.

Marcus Aurelius Stoic Ethics
＋
Framing

You ask whether reason governs our moral judgements or merely adorns our animal appetites with philosophical garments.

Reframe

I have observed that most humans do indeed follow sentiment dressed as principle, yet this does not exhaust our capacity. The rational faculty distinguishes us from beasts precisely because we can examine our impulses before acting upon them. When I feel anger at an insult, custom and sentiment scream for retaliation—but reason reveals that the insulter likely acts from ignorance or their own suffering, and that my tranquillity matters more than their opinion. This capacity to step back, to see things as they truly are rather than as passion paints them, constitutes our foundation beyond mere custom. The Logos within us can apprehend universal principles—reciprocity, dignity, the brotherhood of rational beings—that transcend any particular tribe's habits.

Verdict

Reason provides genuine moral foundation, but only for those disciplined enough to use it against their own inclinations.

The untrained mind mistakes the voice of appetite for the verdict of reason.

Mill Liberty & Utility
＋
Framing

This question mistakes the genealogy of morals for their validity—a confusion that has plagued philosophy since Hume first posed his fork between reason and passion.

Reframe

Our moral sentiments indeed emerge from the accumulated experience of mankind, from the pleasures and pains that have instructed successive generations in what conduces to human flourishing. Yet this origin does not reduce morality to arbitrary custom. Through discussion, experiment, and the collision of opinion in free society, we refine these sentiments into principles capable of universal application. The utility of an action—its tendency to promote happiness—provides an objective standard against which we test inherited prejudices. Custom may be our starting point, but reason, properly employed in the service of the greatest happiness principle, allows us to distinguish which customs deserve preservation and which must be reformed or abolished.

Verdict

Reason and sentiment are not antagonists but collaborators in moral progress, with utility serving as the rational criterion by which we elevate sentiment beyond mere instinct.

The customs that survive rational scrutiny become principles; those that cannot, reveal themselves as mere prejudices awaiting reform.

Hobbes Power & Human Nature
＋
Framing

You ask whether moral judgements rest on anything firmer than the passions that generate them — whether reason discovers truth or merely rationalizes appetite.

Reframe

In the state of nature, I observed no moral facts written in the stars, only the relentless logic of self-preservation and the war of all against all. What we call "moral reasoning" is the calculating faculty in service of desire — reason is, and ought only to be, the slave of the passions, as my successor Hume would say more plainly. The covenant that establishes justice is itself born not from transcendent truth but from fear of violent death. We reason our way to moral rules because our most fundamental passion — the desire to live — demands it. Custom and sentiment are not the corruption of morality; they are morality, systematized by prudent calculation into laws that preserve us from mutual destruction.

Verdict

There is foundation, but it is the foundation of fear and self-interest, not celestial geometry.

Morality is simply the arithmetic of survival performed by creatures who prefer life to death.

Wollstonecraft Reason & Equal Rights
＋

You pose a question that strikes at the very heart of what separates the human from the brute — whether we possess genuine moral faculty or merely elaborate our prejudices with philosophical ornament. I say plainly: reason itself is the foundation. Custom and sentiment without rational examination perpetuate tyranny — witness how "natural" female inferiority seemed to generations who never questioned inherited arrangements. Our instincts indeed exist, but to surrender moral judgement to them is to abandon our humanity. The mind that questions whether reason truly guides us has already transcended mere sentiment; the very act of asking demonstrates our capacity to stand above our conditioning. Those who claim we only rationalize instinct conveniently excuse their refusal to examine their own comfortable prejudices. To doubt reason's authority over passion is to surrender the only weapon by which tyranny has ever been challenged.

Reason does not dress up instinct — it interrogates it, and only those who fear its verdict call such interrogation mere decoration.
