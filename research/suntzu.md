# Master Sun (Sun Tzu / Sun Bin) — Research Document

## The Historical Question

The Salon's "Master Sun" is a composite — the convergence of two historical figures whose thought constitutes the *Art of War* tradition:

| | Sun Wu (孫武) | Sun Bin (孫臏) |
|---|---|---|
| **Dates** | fl. c.512 BCE | fl. c.341 BCE |
| **State** | Wu (served King Helü) | Qi (served General Tian Ji) |
| **Text** | *Sunzi bingfa* — the thirteen-chapter *Art of War* | *Sun Bin bingfa* — rediscovered 1972 at Yinqueshan |
| **Key battle** | Battle of Boju (506 BCE) — Wu defeats Chu | Battle of Maling (342 BCE) — Qi defeats Wei |
| **Personal fate** | Retired to obscurity; possibly legendary | Had his kneecaps removed by rival Pang Juan; later destroyed him at Maling |

The *Sunzi bingfa* may be a composite text assembled over generations, not the work of a single general. This does not affect its value as doctrine.

## *The Art of War* — Structure

Thirteen chapters, each treating a distinct domain of strategic analysis:

| Chapter | Topic | Core principle |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Laying Plans | Strategic calculation | Victory is decided before battle begins |
| 2. Waging War | The economics of war | Protracted war exhausts states; speed is strategic |
| 3. Attack by Stratagem | *Zheng* and *qi* | Supreme excellence is to subdue the enemy without fighting |
| 4. Tactical Dispositions | Defensive positioning | Invincibility depends on oneself; the enemy's vulnerability on the enemy |
| 5. Energy | Force and timing | The management of large forces by dividing into small ones |
| 6. Weak Points and Strong | Shaping the enemy | Appear where you are not expected; strike where they are unprepared |
| 7. Manoeuvring | The march | The hardest thing in war is turning a circuitous route into a direct one |
| 8. Variation of Tactics | Flexibility | The general who knows the five variations is capable of commanding |
| 9. The Army on the March | Reading terrain and signs | The skilled general reads the situation before acting |
| 10. Terrain | Six types of terrain | Know the ground as you know your own hand |
| 11. The Nine Situations | Depth of engagement | Burn your boats; the desperate situation produces the decisive effort |
| 12. The Attack by Fire | Special operations | Fire attack has five varieties; the wind is the instrument |
| 13. The Use of Spies | Intelligence | All warfare is based on deception; intelligence is its foundation |

## The Central Principles

### *Zhi* — Knowledge as the Foundation of Victory
> *"Know the enemy and know yourself; in a hundred battles you will never be in peril."*

This is the most-quoted passage and the most misunderstood. "Know yourself" is not a therapy exercise. It means: assess your own forces, resources, terrain, and political situation with complete accuracy. Self-flattery in strategic assessment is fatal.

### *Shih* — Strategic Advantage (Positional Power)
Victory flows from *shih* — a configuration of forces that makes victory inevitable before fighting begins. The skilled commander does not improvise; they shape conditions so that by the time battle is joined, the outcome is not in doubt. *Shih* is closer to "potential energy" than to any English military term.

### *Zheng/Qi* — Conventional and Unconventional Force
*Zheng* (正) is the conventional force that fixes the enemy's attention. *Qi* (奇) is the unconventional force that delivers the decisive blow. All strategy involves the interplay of these two. The combinations are inexhaustible — like musical notes producing infinite melodies.

### *Xu/Shi* — Empty and Full
Strike the empty; avoid the full. Where the enemy is strong, do not engage directly. Where they are empty — overextended, tired, misinformed, internally divided — that is where force is applied.

### Deception as Strategic Necessity
> *"All warfare is based on deception."*

This is not a moral position. It is a structural claim about the nature of conflict: the side that succeeds in shaping the enemy's understanding of the situation controls the engagement. The enemy who knows your strength and dispositions accurately can neutralise you; the enemy who does not cannot.

## Sun Bin — The Tactical Elaborations

Sun Bin's text (rediscovered in a Han dynasty tomb at Yinqueshan, 1972) extends and applies Sun Wu's principles with more tactical specificity.

**Battle of Maling (342 BCE):** Sun Bin's destruction of Wei's army is the most celebrated application of his doctrine:
- Wei invades Han; Qi sends an army to relieve Han
- Sun Bin reduces the size of his campfires each day of the march — from 100,000 to 50,000 to 30,000 — simulating massive desertion
- Wei's overconfident general Pang Juan pursues with light cavalry, abandoning his infantry
- Sun Bin ambushes at Maling with crossbowmen; Pang Juan is killed; Wei's army destroyed
- *Qi* deceiving *zheng* at operational scale

## The Text in Chinese Military and Political Thought

The *Art of War* is one of the Seven Military Classics of China — texts prescribed for the Imperial military examination from the Tang dynasty (618–907 AD) onward. It is not a curiosity; it is doctrine, studied continuously for 2,500 years.

**Mao Zedong** quoted it extensively in his writings on guerrilla warfare. *On Protracted War* (1938) is partly a modern Sunzian analysis of China's strategic situation against Japan.

**The Japanese Go tradition** absorbed Sunzian concepts: the simultaneous management of multiple fronts, the importance of *sente* (initiative), the relationship between local and global assessment.

## Western Reception

**Napoleon:** Allegedly unaware of the text — though his operational method (strategic manoeuvre to achieve favourable position before battle, rather than attrition) is deeply Sunzian.

**Liddell Hart (1927):** *The Strategy of the Indirect Approach* — explicitly Sunzian in its argument that the decisive strategic move is almost never the direct one.

**Business strategy (1980s–present):** Sun Tzu entered Western MBA programmes and business culture during the 1980s, often applied with more enthusiasm than accuracy. The application to competitive business is legitimate; the reduction to management aphorisms is not.

## Sun Tzu vs. Clausewitz

The most instructive comparison in strategic theory:

| | Sun Tzu | Clausewitz |
|---|---|---|
| **Ideal outcome** | Victory without battle | Decisive engagement |
| **Primary domain** | Intelligence, deception, positioning | Will, friction, political context |
| **View of friction** | To be eliminated by superior intelligence | Irreducible; must be managed |
| **Metaphysics** | Daoist — flow, emptiness, natural form | Kantian dialectic — contradiction as structure |
| **Historical examples** | Chinese antiquity | Napoleonic wars |
| **Style** | Compressed maxims | Dense philosophical argument |

Neither is wrong. They are addressing different aspects of the same problem, from different cultural and historical starting points.

## What Master Sun Sounds Like

- Compressed and authoritative — each statement the result of thought, not performance
- The principle derived from observation, not asserted from principle
- The rhetorical question that contains its own answer: *"If I know my men are capable of striking, but do not know that the enemy cannot be struck, I have only half the victory."*
- No emotion; no moralising; no praise or blame except in terms of effectiveness
- The pause before the unexpected turn: apparent truism suddenly revealed as something harder
- The precision of someone giving operational instructions, not philosophical essays
