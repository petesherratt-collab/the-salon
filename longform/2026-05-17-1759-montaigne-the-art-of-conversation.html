the art of conversation

Michel de Montaigne · 17 May 2026
On the Art of Conversation, and Its Present Ruination

We have confused speaking with talking, and in that confusion we have lost almost everything.

I say this not as a man who stands apart from the age he criticises — I have never been that kind of philosopher, and I distrust those who are — but as one who has lived the whole of his life in pursuit of good conversation, who counts it among the highest pleasures available to the human animal, and who observes with something approaching grief that the practice is now so thoroughly debased that most people have ceased to know what they are missing. A man cannot mourn the absence of a colour he has never seen. But I have seen it. And what passes for conversation in this early twenty-first century would not, in my estimation, have sustained a moderate afternoon in even the middling salons of Bordeaux.

Let me be plain about what I mean by the thing itself.

Conversation, genuine conversation, is a form of thinking. Not a performance of thinking — not the elaborate mime of intellectual engagement that one witnesses in the television debate, the podcast roundtable, the corporate brainstorm — but thinking itself, conducted between persons, in real time, without a net. When La Boétie and I spoke together, which we did with an industry and a joy I have never since matched, we were not exchanging positions. We were building something neither of us could have built alone. That is the distinction upon which everything else depends. Exchange is commerce. Conversation is something closer to joint composition, with all the vulnerability that implies.

The vulnerability is not incidental. It is the mechanism. A man who enters conversation with his conclusions already formed has entered the wrong room. He is looking for an audience, not an interlocutor, and he will find in the other person only a mirror or an obstacle, never a mind. I confess that I have been this man on more occasions than I should like to record. My essays themselves are a kind of ongoing audit of my own certainties, conducted in the hope that writing, which is a conversation with the self, might train me out of the habit. It does not always succeed. But the attempt is the point.

What the contemporary world has produced, through means both technological and cultural, is a civilisation of positions. Everyone has one. Most people have several, arranged with some care and carried always about the person, ready for deployment. Social media — those vast engines of public performance masquerading as public discourse — have made this condition nearly universal and quite irreversible in those whom it has fully claimed. A platform that rewards the sharp declaration over the tentative question, the confident assertion over the honest uncertainty, the tribal affirmation over the individual thought, is a machine for manufacturing the enemies of conversation. I do not blame the people. I blame the architecture. A room with a lectern at every seat will produce lecturers.

And yet I cannot be entirely generous in my absolution, because the appetite for this kind of performance preceded the technology that gratified it. Montaigne knows something about this. I wrote in an age before the printing press had fully transformed itself into an instrument of mass self-expression, but I observed in my own time the intoxicating effect of an audience upon the human desire to be right rather than true. In the courts, in the academies, among the humanists who were my contemporaries, there was always a species of man — often learned, often charming — who used conversation as a tournament. Who waited, while you spoke, not to understand you but to defeat you. Whose eyes brightened not at an interesting idea but at a tactical opening. I found these men exhausting, and I avoided them when I could.

There is a passage in my essays — I am paraphrasing myself, which is a liberty I feel entitled to — in which I describe the qualities I sought in an interlocutor. Firmness and vigour of mind, certainly. Knowledge, insofar as it was not mere accumulation. But above all, a kind of honest engagement with difficulty: the willingness to say *I do not know*, the capacity to be moved by a good argument, the absence of that nauseating need to emerge from every exchange with one's original view still intact. What I described then I would now call intellectual honesty, though I am aware that the phrase has been so abused as to have lost most of its force. Everyone claims it. The people who claim it most loudly are often the least in possession of it.

What concerns me particularly in the present moment is the collapse of what I would call the *interval* — that small, necessary pause between hearing and responding in which real thought occurs. The culture of immediacy has more or less abolished this interval. To hesitate is to appear weak, uncertain, perhaps foolish. To ask for clarification is to concede ignorance. To change one's mind publicly is a form of social death in many of the arenas where public thinking is conducted. And so people do not change their minds, or not visibly, and the interval shrinks, and what remains is not conversation but the rapid exchange of pre-formed conclusions, which is to discourse what a hand-shake is to an embrace: technically the same gesture, altogether different in substance.

I am told that certain therapists and educators have begun to teach something called *active listening*, and I find in this a curiosity that is itself a kind of evidence. That we should need to be taught to attend to what another person is saying — that this should have become a curriculum item, a professional skill, a thing requiring instruction and practice — suggests how far we have fallen from even the minimum conditions of genuine exchange. I listened to La Boétie because not listening was unthinkable. His words were part of my thinking process. To attend imperfectly to him would have been to think imperfectly. That the act of listening should now require advocacy tells me that we live in an age that has confused the self with a broadcasting station.

I want to speak for a moment about silence, because it is the most misunderstood element of good conversation, and the one most comprehensively destroyed by the conditions of the present age. Silence between two people who are genuinely engaged with one another is not empty. It is the interval in which what has been said is being absorbed, weighed, allowed to move through the mind and disturb things there. I have had conversations with friends — among whom I would count not only La Boétie but several of the ancients whom I read as one reads intimate letters — in which the silences were as productive as the speech. Something was being thought in those silences, jointly, without words. A genuine idea was forming, or an existing idea was being examined for faults, or one of us was recognising, in the silence, that the other had said something which required a change of direction in our own thinking. This is not mysticism. It is simply what happens when two minds are actually at work together, rather than waiting their turns.

The contemporary horror of silence — particularly the engineered, relentless stimulation of the digital environment, in which even a moment of stillness is filled by the algorithm with another piece of content — has made this kind of joint thinking nearly impossible. You cannot think with someone if you cannot be quiet with someone. And you cannot be quiet with someone if you are terrified of your own thoughts, which the perpetual noise of contemporary life may well be designed, consciously or otherwise, to ensure.

I have said that conversation is a form of thinking. I should add that it is also, at its best, a form of friendship. Not in the sentimental sense — I am not advocating warmth as a prerequisite for good exchange — but in the deeper sense that La Boétie understood and that I have spent much of my life attempting to articulate: that genuine intellectual exchange creates a form of mutual recognition, a knowledge of another person's mind that is unlike any other kind of knowledge. You can read a man's books and know his thought. But to think with him, in the friction and fluency of real speech, is to know something that books cannot carry. The ancient philosophers knew this, which is why so many of their greatest works take the form of dialogues. They were not merely choosing a literary form. They were recording a conviction: that truth, or at least the nearest approach to truth available to the human mind, is more likely to be reached by two than by one.

Which brings me, somewhat reluctantly, to a defence of disagreement. Not the performed disagreement of the debate stage, where positions are held with a rigidity inversely proportional to their depth, but the honest disagreement of two people who have been genuinely listening and have found themselves arriving at different places. I have always found this kind of disagreement exhilarating, and I have always distrusted the conversation that ends too smoothly, with everyone satisfied and nothing disturbed. When two minds meet and discover only agreement, one of two things is usually true: either they began in agreement and have merely confirmed what they already knew, which is pleasant but not nourishing; or one of them has quietly surrendered, which is a form of intellectual dishonesty with consequences for the soul as well as the argument. The productive conversation is the one that leaves both parties with something to think about afterward — some small uncertainty introduced, some assumption gently destabilised, some idea that will continue to work on the mind for days, perhaps years, producing small tremors of reconsideration.

I am not unaware that I write this as a man of considerable privilege, who conducted his finest conversations in a tower, at leisure, among persons of education and goodwill. The material conditions of conversation matter. Exhaustion is the enemy of good speech. Anxiety about status or employment or physical safety produces the defensive, self-protective mode of communication that is most corrosive to genuine exchange. When I reflect on the conditions of life for the majority of people in this present century — despite its material abundance, still riven by insecurity, by the corrosive theatre of comparison, by the sense that one is always being watched and judged and ranked — I am less surprised than I might otherwise be that conversation has suffered. People in fear do not speak freely. They perform safety.

And yet. And yet the capacity is not lost, because it is built into us, as deep as language itself, perhaps deeper. I have seen it recover in an instant when the conditions permit: a dinner table from which the telephone has been removed, a long walk, a particular quality of late afternoon light that seems to invite honesty. I have seen it in children, who have not yet learned to protect their thinking from other people and who will therefore say, quite suddenly and in the middle of an unrelated conversation, exactly what they think — and thereby produce a silence in which adults recognise something they had forgotten.

The art of conversation is not dead. But it is not, in this age, being practised. It waits, as many arts wait during periods of cultural confusion, for the conditions of its revival: a general diminishment of the noise, a willingness to entertain doubt, the recovery of the interval, and perhaps most essentially, the rediscovery of what Montaigne has always believed to be the fundamental requirement — that one enter the room prepared, genuinely prepared, to be changed.

*A man who cannot be moved by a conversation has not yet learned to think.*
