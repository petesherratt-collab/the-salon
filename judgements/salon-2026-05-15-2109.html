The Question If we accept that all knowledge comes through experience, how do we account for those moral truths which seem to announce themselves in consciousness prior to any worldly instruction?

📜
Montaigne Self & Society
＋

The question assumes a certainty I have never felt. When I examine my conscience most honestly, I find no moral truths that arrived unsullied by custom, climate, and the particular accidents of my upbringing. What announces itself as innate conviction is often merely the earliest education — so early we mistake it for nature itself. The child who recoils from cruelty has already absorbed a thousand lessons in his mother's tone, his father's approvals, the stories whispered at bedtime. We are creatures of imitation before we are creatures of reason. Even those moral sentiments that feel most universal — compassion for suffering, perhaps — vary so remarkably across peoples and epochs that I cannot grant them the privilege of being self-evident. Experience writes upon us from the first breath; we simply forget the penmanship. My verdict: What seems prior to instruction is merely instruction we have forgotten receiving.

We mistake the antiquity of our learning for its innateness.

Nietzsche Will & Value

Framing

You pose the empiricist's riddle as though it were still worth solving — as if "moral truths" were discoveries rather than creations, things we find rather than legislate.

Reframe

What you call truths "announcing themselves in consciousness" are the accumulated judgements of countless ancestors speaking through your blood and nerves. These so-called a priori moral intuitions are experience compressed into instinct — the digested wisdom of millennia of tribal survival, now mistaken for divine revelation or rational self-evidence. The herd's "thou shalt" has become your inner voice, and you call this consciousness rather than conditioning. What seems to precede experience is merely experience so ancient it has become physiology.

Verdict

There are no moral truths independent of experience; there are only valuations so deeply inherited they feel like revelations.

What announces itself as conscience is merely the ventriloquism of dead generations speaking through your living flesh.

Hume Scepticism & Human Nature
＋
Framing

You mistake the feeling of immediacy for temporal priority—moral sentiments strike us with such force that we fancy them innate, when they are merely habits worn smooth by repetition.

Reframe

What appears as conscience "announcing itself" is actually the residue of a thousand small experiences: a mother's approval, a companion's distress, the pleasure of reciprocated kindness. These accumulate below the threshold of memory until the sentiment seems to spring from nowhere. I have never observed a moral truth that does not trace back to the passions—sympathy above all—which are themselves refined by social commerce. The child who has never witnessed suffering does not recoil from cruelty by divine implantation; he learns revulsion through feeling.

Verdict

Your "prior" moral truths are posterior experiences mistaken for first principles because custom has rendered their origins invisible.

What custom has made instant, pride mistakes for eternal.

Adam Smith Markets & Moral Sentiment
＋
Framing

You ask how moral truths arise unbidden in the mind if experience alone instructs us — this is the puzzle of our immediate sympathies.

Reframe

I observe that the moral sentiments do not wait upon philosophical demonstration. When we witness cruelty, indignation rises in our breast before we have consulted any treatise. Yet this is not evidence of innate ideas inscribed upon the soul, but rather the operation of imagination working upon the most universal of all experiences — our own embodied existence. The child who burns his finger learns pain; the same child, observing another's distress, re-creates that sensation through sympathetic imagination. This fellow-feeling is indeed prior to formal instruction, but it rests entirely upon the foundational experience of being a creature capable of pleasure and pain. We carry within us the experimental apparatus for all moral knowledge.

Verdict

Moral truths announce themselves early because sympathy processes our most primitive experiences, not because they transcend experience altogether.

The heart's immediate judgements are not heaven-sent, but experience distilled through the alchemy of fellow-feeling.
