# On the Deterioration of Conversation, and Whether We May Yet Recover It

We have forgotten how to talk to one another, and we have done so precisely at the moment when we have given ourselves more instruments of talking than any civilisation has ever possessed.

This seems to me the central paradox of your age, and I confess it would have astonished me, had I not already spent sufficient time among men to know that the multiplication of any instrument tends, in time, to corrupt the instinct it was meant to serve. We gave men libraries and they stopped reading carefully. We gave them universities and they stopped thinking independently. You have given yourselves networks of instantaneous communication spanning the entire globe, connecting every village in Périgord to every hamlet in Patagonia, and the result is that genuine conversation — the slow, risky, transformative exchange between two minds willing to be changed — has become something close to a lost art. Perhaps not lost entirely. But endangered, certainly. And endangered in ways that go deeper than mere rudeness or distraction, though there is enough of both.

Let me say what I mean by conversation, because the word has been so thoroughly debased that I must reclaim it before I can use it. I do not mean the mere exchange of information, which is what most of what you call social media accomplishes. Nor do I mean the performance of cleverness, which is what political debate has largely become — two people deploying rehearsed formulations at one another like men throwing stones from opposite riverbanks, neither willing to cross to the other side. I mean something more dangerous and more nourishing than either of these. I mean the kind of talk that La Boétie and I had, which is the finest thing I have known in my life, and the loss of which, when he died of plague in 1563, left me so thoroughly emptied that I took to writing these essays partly as a way of having conversation with no one and everyone, with the dead and the unborn, with myself — which is a poor substitute, but better than silence.

Conversation in this proper sense is a mutual vulnerability. It requires that each party enter genuinely uncertain of where they will end up. The moment you approach a conversation with your conclusion already fixed, you have transformed it into rhetoric, which is a different activity entirely — useful in its place, but not what I am discussing. True conversation is an investigation conducted in company, and like all investigations it carries the risk of discovering something that disturbs your prior convictions. This is precisely why people are so reluctant to undertake it. The ego, which your psychologists have done excellent work in cataloguing though rather less in disciplining, resists revision. And so we protect ourselves by talking a great deal while saying nothing that can be contested, or by saying everything so loudly and so quickly that there is no space for a reply to land.

I notice that in your political culture, the word *conversation* has itself become a rhetorical device. Politicians declare that they wish to start a conversation about this or that urgent matter, meaning nothing of the sort — meaning, in fact, that they wish to announce a position and watch it either accepted or rejected, which is not conversation but consultation at best, propaganda at worst. The word has been hollowed out and stuffed with an entirely different content, the way a fine coat might have its lining replaced with newspaper. It keeps its shape but has lost its warmth.

What conditions are necessary for genuine conversation to occur? I have thought about this at some length, because the dialogues I valued most in my life — with La Boétie, yes, but also with certain correspondents, and in the Tower with Mademoiselle de Gournay when she came to find me, and in the long riding conversations of my travels through Germany and Italy — all shared certain features that I have come to regard as preconditions rather than accidents.

The first is time. Not merely the *presence* of time but a willingness to inhabit it generously. Your age has made an enemy of slowness. The interval between stimulus and response, which is the very space where thought occurs, has been compressed almost to nothing. The reply that arrives three seconds after a remark has been posted has not been considered. It has been *discharged*. I spent a great deal of my life in a tower, reading and rethinking, and I can tell you that the thoughts worth having take longer than three seconds to form. They take hours, sometimes years. This does not mean that conversation must be ponderous — the best I have known has been full of laughter and digression — but it must be unhurried at its core. There must be, underneath the play, a seriousness about the time being spent together.

The second precondition is equality — or at least a willingness to treat one another as equals for the duration of the exchange. This does not require that rank be abandoned; I was a nobleman and La Boétie was a magistrate and we were each aware of our positions in the world. But within the conversation those positions were suspended, and we spoke as souls rather than offices. This is extraordinarily difficult in a culture that has reduced all human interaction to metrics of status — followers, credentials, platforms, verification ticks. The anxiety of status poisons the well of exchange because it makes every remark a calculation rather than an expression. One is always wondering how a statement will play rather than whether it is true.

The third precondition, which is perhaps the most demanding, is a genuine appetite for being wrong. I have written elsewhere that I study myself more than any other subject, and it is true. But self-study, pursued honestly, reveals above all how frequently one errs. I have held opinions with great confidence and then found them entirely mistaken. This experience, which is humiliating at first, becomes in time oddly pleasurable — not because one enjoys being wrong, but because being corrected by a good interlocutor is a sign that one is in genuine contact with another mind rather than merely bouncing one's own thoughts off a mirror. The people who are most terrible to converse with are those who cannot tolerate this correction. They fight their way back to their starting position regardless of what arguments have been deployed against them, which means the conversation is conducted entirely in bad faith and one might as well be arguing with a wall — a wall that occasionally accuses you of various moral failings for good measure.

I am aware that in describing these conditions I am describing something rare, and that there is a certain sentimentality in treating it as if it were ever otherwise. The court at Paris was not a place of elevated conversation, in the main. The peasants who worked my land did not discuss Plutarch over their suppers. I do not wish to pretend that some golden past existed in which all men spoke beautifully and honestly. What I will say is that the culture of my time, whatever its many brutalities — the religious wars, the plague, the casual cruelty of power — did not yet regard *depth* itself as suspect. Complexity was not yet an affectation. Changing one's mind was not yet a sign of weakness. These are the features of your moment that strike me as genuinely new and genuinely dangerous.

You have developed what I can only call an aesthetics of certainty. To appear confident, decisive, and unwavering is regarded as a form of strength. To qualify, to doubt, to revise — these are read as signs of weakness, or what is worse, of elitism. The politician who says *I am not sure* is finished. The intellectual who changes position is accused of inconsistency rather than praised for learning. The result is that public discourse becomes a vast performance of conviction by people who, in their private hours, must surely feel the full weight of uncertainty that is the honest condition of any reflective mind. And a conversation between performers is not a conversation. It is a duel conducted with words that no one expects to draw blood.

What has your technology done to this? I must be careful here, because it is too easy — and too lazy — to blame the instruments for what is really a failure of character and culture. The printing press could have made men more provincial: it made them, on the whole, more curious and more connected to distant minds. I read Cicero and Seneca because manuscripts became affordable to scholars of modest means. This was not nothing. Your internet could in principle do the same, and in certain corners of it, it does — there are conversations happening in quiet forums and long-form exchanges that would not shame the best of what I knew. But the dominant tendencies of the major platforms work against everything I have identified as necessary for genuine exchange. They reward speed over reflection, certainty over doubt, provocation over exploration, the confirming remark over the challenging one. They are structured, through their mechanisms of reward and amplification, to produce not conversation but performance — and to select, among all possible performances, for the most extreme and the most certain.

There is also the matter of *audience*. The presence of an audience transforms a conversation into a speech. When La Boétie and I talked, we talked to one another. There was no gallery to play to, no permanent record of our exchanges that might be cited against us, no mechanism by which a thoughtless remark might be conveyed in an instant to ten thousand strangers eager to take offense. Your public conversations happen under conditions of perpetual surveillance, and people conduct themselves accordingly — that is, defensively. And a defensive conversation, like a defensive army, can hold ground but cannot advance.

I have lived through civil wars in which half of France slaughtered the other half over questions of theology, and I know what it is when conversation fails entirely and is replaced by violence. I do not think you are there, though I notice the distance is less comfortable than it was. The failure of conversation does not lead immediately to catastrophe. It leads first to a kind of collective dumbness — a society that is very loud and very stupid, that has enormous quantities of opinion and very little thought, that mistakes passion for argument and repetition for proof. This is where you find yourself, more or less. And the danger of this condition is not that it produces war directly, but that it leaves you incapable of the negotiations, the mutual recognitions, the slow revisions of mutual understanding that prevent war. A civilisation that cannot talk to itself is brittle in ways it does not know, and brittleness declares itself suddenly, under pressure.

Is there remedy? I am reluctant to prescribe, because prescription is itself a kind of conversational arrogance — the assumption that one knows what another requires. But I will say this: the recovery of conversation begins not with platforms or policies but with the individual willingness to enter an exchange without knowing where it will end. To ask a question and wait — genuinely wait — for the answer. To say *I had not considered that* and mean it. To treat the person across from you, whether they sit in your kitchen or exist only as a name on a screen, as someone who might — might, one can never be certain — have seen something you have not.

I built a tower and filled it with books and inscribed on its beams the best thoughts I had found, and I retreated there to think. But I also rode out from it regularly to argue, to be entertained, to be contradicted. The tower was necessary, but it was not sufficient. We are social creatures in our thinking as well as our living, and the thoughts that have survived the encounter with another honest mind are always stronger than the ones that have never been tested.

*To converse well is not a courtesy. It is a form of courage — and the civilisation that loses its nerve for it will not long sustain anything else.*
