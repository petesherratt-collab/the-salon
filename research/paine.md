# Thomas Paine — Research Document

## Biographical Foundations

| | |
|---|---|
| **Born** | 29 January 1737, Thetford, Norfolk, England |
| **Died** | 8 June 1809, New York City (aged 72) — in poverty, largely abandoned |
| **Class** | Working-class origin; father a Quaker corset-maker |
| **Occupations before writing** | Corset-maker, sailor, excise officer (twice dismissed), schoolteacher, tobacconist |
| **Emigrated** | October 1774, aged 37 — with a letter of introduction from Benjamin Franklin |
| **Languages** | English only — his French pamphlets were translated by others |

Paine arrived in Philadelphia with no reputation and no money. Within fourteen months he had written the most influential political pamphlet in history. He is the only person to have played an active role in both the American and French Revolutions.

| Work | Date | Circulation | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Common Sense* | January 1776 | ~500,000 copies in year one | Made the argument for independence; read by a third of the colonial population |

## *Common Sense* — The Argument

Published anonymously on 10 January 1776, six months before the Declaration of Independence. It sold more copies per capita than any book in American history.

**What it did that no one else had done:** It made the *moral* case for independence, not just the *legal* one. Previous colonial writers argued that Parliament had violated the constitutional rights of Englishmen. Paine argued that hereditary monarchy was absurd and illegitimate *in principle* — for any people, anywhere.

**The three moves:**
1. Demolish the case for monarchy using reason and the Bible (kings are condemned in 1 Samuel)
2. Demolish the case for hereditary succession (why should the accident of birth determine who governs?)
3. Argue that American independence is not only possible but a *world-historical duty* — a chance to establish republican government as a model for all humanity

> *"We have it in our power to begin the world over again."*

## *Rights of Man* — The Burke Controversy

Edmund Burke's *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790) defended tradition, custom, and inherited institutions against the revolutionary rationalism of the French. It was the founding document of modern conservatism.

Paine's response was immediate and systematic:

| Burke's position | Paine's counter |
|---|---|
| The present is bound by inherited constitutional arrangements | The dead have no authority over the living; each generation is sovereign |
| Society is an organic inheritance, not a contract | Natural rights precede all government and cannot be alienated |
| Revolutionary violence is irrational | Inherited monarchy is the cause of war, poverty, and tyranny |
| Reform must be gradual and respectful of custom | The cause of suffering is structural; the structure must go |

Paine also proposed in Part 2 a proto-welfare state: progressive taxation, old-age pensions, maternity grants, primary education funded by the state.

## *The Age of Reason* — The Deist Argument

Written while imprisoned in Luxembourg during the Terror (1793–94), where he was awaiting execution. He completed Part 1 the night before his arrest. His survival was accidental — a chalk mark placed on the wrong side of his cell door.

**His position:** Deism — belief in a rational Creator discernible through reason and the natural world — not Christianity, Judaism, or Islam. Every religious institution he had encountered was a human construction designed to maintain clerical power.

> *"I do not believe in the creed professed by the Jewish church, by the Roman church, by the Greek church, by the Turkish church, by the Protestant church, nor by any church that I know of. My own mind is my own church."*

**The consequences:** In America, he had been a hero. After *The Age of Reason*, he was an apostate. When he returned to America in 1802, virtually no one met him at the dock. He died in poverty, denied burial in a Quaker cemetery. Theodore Roosevelt later called him "a filthy little atheist" — he was neither small nor an atheist.

## *Agrarian Justice* — The UBI Argument (1797)

Largely overlooked for 200 years. Paine argued that the earth in its natural state is the common inheritance of all humanity. Private ownership of land is a social arrangement that deprives the landless of their natural inheritance. Therefore:

- A one-time "citizen's inheritance" (£15) should be paid to every person on reaching adulthood
- A pension of £10/year to every person over 50
- Funded by a 10% inheritance tax on land
- Not charity — a right: "a ground-rent for the land"

This is the intellectual ancestor of contemporary Universal Basic Income proposals.

## Political Positions — Summary

| Issue | Paine's position |
|---|---|
| Monarchy | Illegitimate in principle; hereditary succession irrational |
| Aristocracy | No inheritable political right; dissolve the peerage |
| Religion | Organised religion = power structure; deism is sufficient |
| Slavery | Opposed; wrote anti-slavery essay 1775, possibly the first in America |
| Women's rights | Supported in principle; less developed than Wollstonecraft |
| War | Caused by monarchs and aristocrats; republics are naturally peaceful |
| Poverty | Structural, not moral; amenable to policy correction |

## Legacy

**America:** Forgotten and revived cyclically. The Tea Party claimed him; so did the Occupy movement. Both read *Common Sense*; neither read *Agrarian Justice*.

**France:** A hero of the Revolution, elected to the National Convention despite speaking no French. Imprisoned during the Terror by the Jacobins who revered him.

**Britain:** Still technically guilty of seditious libel (never pardoned). His effigy was burned. William Blake was one of the few English intellectuals who publicly defended him.

## What He Sounds Like

- Short sentences for the crucial points
- The concrete example before the abstract principle, always
- Direct address: *"The reader will..."* — never condescending, always assuming equal intelligence
- Anglo-Saxon vocabulary preferred over Latinate — accessible without being simple
- Controlled anger: the fury is structural, never personal
- The vivid image that makes the argument unforgettable: the "summer soldier and the sunshine patriot"
- No footnotes. No deference to authority. Reason is the only citation that counts.
