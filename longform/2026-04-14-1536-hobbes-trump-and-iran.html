Trump and Iran

Hobbes · 14 April 2026
On the Conduct of Trump Toward Iran, and What It Reveals Concerning the Nature of Sovereign Power in Modern States

Let us begin with first principles, as is my custom, for I find that most political confusion arises not from the complexity of present circumstances but from the failure to define terms and establish foundations. The matter of Trump and Iran — by which I understand the question of how the American sovereign conducted himself toward the Persian state during his tenure, and what consequences followed — cannot be properly understood without first clarifying what we mean by sovereignty, what constitutes rational state action, and what distinguishes policy from caprice.

I. On Sovereignty and the Modern American Condition

The United States of America, despite its republican pretensions and its elaborate distribution of powers among executive, legislative, and judicial branches, functions in matters of foreign policy very nearly as I prescribed a sovereign ought to function: with unified will, decisive authority, and the capacity to make war and peace. The American president, in the conduct of foreign affairs, exercises a power that approaches — though never quite achieves — the absoluteness I deem necessary for the maintenance of civil peace. This is not accident but necessity. A commonwealth cannot negotiate with foreign powers through committee, nor can it respond to threats with the dilatory proceedings of assemblies. In these matters, decision must be swift, and swiftness requires unity of command.

Trump, then, came to power as the American sovereign in all matters touching foreign relations. That he was constrained by domestic institutions, by courts and congress and the machinery of permanent administration, is true; but in the specific matter of Iran, he possessed what I should call functional sovereignty — the capacity to act decisively and to commit the commonwealth to a course of action.

II. The State of Nature Between Commonwealths

Let us be clear about what obtains between sovereign states: it is the state of nature. Within a commonwealth, men surrender their natural liberty to the sovereign in exchange for peace and protection. But between commonwealths, there exists no common power, no sovereign of sovereigns, no authority that can effectively arbitrate disputes or enforce covenants. The so-called "international order" or "rules-based system" which modern men speak of with such reverence is nothing but a polite fiction masking the fundamental truth that states remain in relation to each other as men would be without government — in a posture of war, actual or potential.

This does not mean states must always be fighting. Men in the state of nature might form temporary alliances, make contracts of mutual advantage, and even maintain long periods of peace. But these arrangements rest not on justice — for there is no justice without law, and no law without a sovereign to enforce it — but on calculation of interest and balance of power. The covenant between states is worth precisely what it costs to break it, measured against the benefits of keeping it.

The arrangement between the United States and Iran prior to Trump's accession — the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action, as it was styled — was such a covenant. Iran pledged to limit its nuclear program in exchange for relief from economic sanctions. The agreement was witnessed by other powers and incorporated into resolutions of the United Nations, but let no man be deceived: its binding force derived not from these formalities but from the calculation by each party that maintaining the agreement served its interest better than breaking it.

III. Trump's Withdrawal from the Covenant

In the year 2018, Trump withdrew the United States from this agreement. His stated reasons were several: that the agreement was insufficient in its restrictions upon Iran; that it failed to address Iran's development of ballistic missiles or its conduct throughout the region; that it would eventually permit Iran to pursue nuclear weapons without constraint; and — most tellingly — that it was a "bad deal," negotiated from weakness, shameful to American interests.

From the perspective of strict analysis, we must ask: was this withdrawal rational? Did it serve the interest of the commonwealth? Here we encounter the fundamental problem of Trump's mode of sovereignty: the difficulty of distinguishing policy from impulse, calculation from gesture, the pursuit of the commonwealth's safety from the pursuit of personal vindication.

The case for withdrawal rested on the proposition that maximum economic pressure — the reimposition of severe sanctions — would compel Iran either to negotiate a more comprehensive agreement or to collapse under the weight of economic distress. This is not an absurd theory. Economic power is indeed a form of power, and the American capacity to exclude nations from global commerce through its control of financial systems is considerable. If one seeks to cow an adversary, the demonstration of willingness to inflict pain may serve where patience appears as weakness.

But observe what followed. Iran did not collapse. Its people suffered — as they always do when rulers make war upon each other, whether with weapons or with embargoes — but its regime did not fall. Nor did Iran offer concessions; rather, it resumed the very nuclear activities the agreement had constrained, calculated that it had nothing further to lose by doing so, and increased its regional activities. The American action, intended to strengthen its position, had in effect weakened it. The agreement, imperfect as it may have been, had at least placed monitors within Iran and limits upon its nuclear program. Withdrawn from the agreement, the United States retained the costs — the enmity of Iran, the distrust of European allies who opposed the withdrawal — while losing the benefits.

IV. The Killing of Soleimani

In January of 2020, Trump ordered the killing of Qasem Soleimani, a Persian general, by means of an aerial strike as he departed the airport at Baghdad. This act requires examination, for it reveals much about the nature of Trump's exercise of sovereign power.

The killing of an enemy commander is not, in itself, contrary to the law of nature between commonwealths. In the state of war — and the United States and Iran, though not engaged in declared war, had long been in a state of hostility approaching war — each party has the right to inflict what damage it can upon the other, limited only by calculation of consequence and the residual customs that men call the laws of war. Soleimani had, by American account, been responsible for actions that killed American soldiers and threatened American interests throughout the region. His killing might be justified as an act of war against an enemy combatant.

But consider the manner and context of this action. It was not preceded by any formal declaration. It was not part of a sustained campaign of military action. It was sudden, spectacular, and executed with such precision as to demonstrate American capability without demonstrating American purpose. The question that must be asked of any act of state is: quo tendis? Whither tends this action? What end does it serve? What consequences has it been calculated to produce?

If the purpose was deterrence — to make Iran fear the cost of further actions against American interests — then we must ask whether this effect was achieved. Iran did indeed retaliate, striking American bases in Iraq and placing its forces on alert, but the escalation stopped short of open war. Some would call this success: a message delivered, revenge satisfied on both sides, and hostilities contained. Perhaps. But perhaps also what occurred was not rational calculation on either side but mere fortune — the good luck that neither party, having stumbled to the brink, was quite mad enough to leap over it.

If the purpose was to degrade Iranian capability by removing a competent commander, this too may have been achieved, though commanders can be replaced and martyrs are more dangerous dead than alive. What was certainly achieved was the elevation of Soleimani to the status of national hero, the unification of Persian sentiment against the United States, and the passage of a resolution by the Iraqi parliament demanding the removal of American forces from their territory — hardly the intended consequence, one assumes, of an action taken on Iraqi soil.

V. On Consistency and Credibility in the Conduct of Sovereigns

Here we arrive at the central defect of Trump's mode of sovereignty: the absence of consistent policy pursued toward consistent ends. A sovereign may be severe or merciful, aggressive or conciliatory, but he must be predictable in his principles. Other states must be able to calculate what will provoke him and what will appease him, what he values and what he despises, what he will fight for and what he will concede. This is not a matter of kindness — I have never counseled kindness in princes — but of rational self-interest. The credibility of threats and promises is the currency of international relations, and credibility requires consistency.

Trump's conduct toward Iran displayed no such consistency. He withdrew from an agreement on the grounds that Iran could not be trusted, then repeatedly expressed willingness to negotiate a new agreement with the same regime he declared could not be trusted. He imposed "maximum pressure," then refrained from maximum action when Iran tested him — shooting down American drones, attacking ships, striking Saudi oil facilities — until suddenly and without clear pattern he ordered the killing of Soleimani. He declared that he did not seek war, then acted in ways calculated to risk war, then pulled back from the brink.

This is not the calculated ambiguity of a clever strategist keeping his enemies uncertain. It is the incoherence of policy formed by impulse, by the stimulus of television commentary, by personal grievance and personal vanity. Other nations — adversaries and allies alike — could not calculate what America would do because America's sovereign could not himself say what he would do until the moment of doing it.

VI. The Failure of Maximum Pressure as Strategy

Let us examine the theory of maximum pressure with the cold eye of one who studies power rather than wishes. The assumption underlying Trump's approach was that economic suffering would compel either the collapse of the Iranian regime or its capitulation to American demands. But this assumption rests on a misunderstanding of how regimes fall and how they persist.

A regime falls when it loses the capacity to coerce obedience — when its armies will not fight, its police will not enforce, its administrators will not administer. Economic hardship may contribute to this dissolution, but only if it affects those who hold power or if it creates such widespread misery that even the instruments of coercion rebel. Sanctions imposed from without typically strengthen rather than weaken regimes, for they provide a visible external enemy to blame for internal suffering, they create opportunities for those who control smuggling and black markets, and they make the population more dependent on the state for survival.

Iran's regime, moreover, had been born in revolution against a government seen as subservient to foreign powers. Its legitimating narrative was one of resistance to Western domination, particularly American domination. Economic pressure from America therefore reinforced rather than undermined the regime's claim to represent Persian independence and dignity. The suffering of the people could be — and was — attributed to American cruelty rather than to the government's policies.

As for compelling Iran to negotiate better terms: why should it? From Iran's perspective, America had already demonstrated that it would not honor agreements. Trump had withdrawn from a covenant that Iran, by all accounts, had been observing. What value could there be in negotiating a new agreement with a power that treated agreements as mere scraps of paper, to be discarded when convenient? The very action that Trump took to demonstrate American strength — the withdrawal from the agreement — destroyed the foundation for achieving what he claimed to seek.

VII. On Allies and the Credibility of the Sovereign

A power cannot act in the world alone, not even a power as great as the United States. It requires allies, bases, intelligence, the cooperation of other states in enforcing its will. Trump's approach to Iran damaged American relations with its European allies, who had been parties to the nuclear agreement and who opposed its termination.

Some would say this matters not at all — that a great power need not concern itself with the opinions of lesser powers. But this is to misunderstand the nature of power in the modern world. American sanctions work because European and Asian companies fear being excluded from American markets. American military action succeeds because it can operate from bases in allied countries. American intelligence comes partly from allied services. The appearance of American action as unilateral diktat rather than as the will of an alliance reduces its effectiveness and increases its cost.

Moreover, the treatment of allies signals to adversaries how the American sovereign regards obligations. If America withdraws from an agreement with Iran despite the objections of its allies, what signal does this send to those allies about the value of their own agreements with America? Every sovereign must at times break covenants when necessity requires it — I am no sentimentalist about the sanctity of promises between states — but the manner of breaking matters. To break an agreement suddenly, without consultation, while declaring that the terms were never acceptable, is to announce oneself as unreliable in all dealings.

VIII. The Question of Deterrence and Resolve

There remains the argument that Trump's approach, however chaotic it may have appeared, succeeded in deterring Iranian action through the demonstration of unpredictability and willingness to use force. After the killing of Soleimani, Iran's response was carefully calibrated to avoid American casualties; the missile strikes on American bases were preceded by warnings that allowed personnel to take shelter. Does this not suggest that Iran had been taught to fear American retaliation?

Perhaps. Or perhaps both sides, having escalated to the edge of war, recognized that neither truly wanted war and stepped back by mutual unspoken agreement. Deterrence is always a matter of interpretation. Was Iran deterred by Trump's demonstration of resolve, or was Trump deterred by Iran's demonstration that it would not be intimidated without cost? Both can claim the same sequence of events as evidence of their success, which suggests that the sequence proves little.

What can be said with certainty is that Iran's nuclear program advanced further during Trump's tenure than it had under the agreement he terminated. Iran enriched uranium to higher levels, installed more advanced centrifuges, and reduced cooperation with international monitors. If the purpose of Trump's policy was to prevent Iran from acquiring nuclear weapons capability, it failed. The agreement he rejected as insufficient had at least delayed this outcome; his alternative accelerated it.

IX. On the Distinction Between Appearing Strong and Being Strong

Trump was much concerned with appearing strong — with demonstrations of power, with refusing to appear weak, with what he perceived as the restoration of American dominance. This is not entirely foolish; the reputation of power is itself a form of power, and a sovereign who appears weak invites challenges. But there is a difference between the appearance of strength and its substance, and Trump often confused the two.

True strength lies in the capacity to achieve one's ends, not in the magnitude of the gestures one makes. A sovereign who kills an enemy general achieves a momentary satisfaction, perhaps, and a demonstration of technical capability. But if this action brings him no closer to his objectives — if it neither compels his enemy to yield nor degrades the enemy's capacity to resist — then it is spectacle rather than strategy, performance rather than policy.

The withdrawal from the nuclear agreement was presented as strength — as refusing to accept a bad deal, as showing that America would not be bound by the weak commitments of a previous administration. But what is strong about trading a functioning arrangement that constrained one's enemy for no arrangement at all? What is strong about isolating oneself from one's allies in pursuit of a better deal that never materializes? The appearance of decisive action is not the same as the substance of effective action.

X. The Madman Theory and Its Limits

Some defenders of Trump's approach invoke what is called the madman theory — the notion that a leader who appears irrational or unpredictable gains advantage because adversaries cannot calculate his actions and therefore fear to provoke him. There is a grain of truth in this. If an enemy believes you might do anything, including things contrary to your own interest, he may be deterred from testing you.

But this theory has severe limits. First, the appearance of madness is useful only if it masks underlying rationality — if the madman is in fact carefully calculating the effects of his apparent irrationality. Random action is not strategic unpredictability. Second, the madman theory works only if the other party is rational enough to be deterred by uncertainty. If both parties appear mad, the result is not deterrence but chaos. Third, the appearance of madness destroys the value of commitments and promises. If no one can believe what you say or predict what you will do, then you cannot coordinate action with allies or offer credible inducements to enemies.

Trump's defenders would have him be a cunning madman, appearing irrational to keep enemies off balance while pursuing carefully calculated goals. But the evidence suggests something closer to actual inconsistency — policy driven by mood, by the last person who spoke to him, by cable news commentary, by personal grievance. This produces not strategic unpredictability but simple unreliability, which is a weakness rather than a strength.

XI. On the Permanent State and the Limits of Personal Sovereignty

Trump frequently complained that his policies toward Iran and other matters were undermined by what he termed "the deep state" — by permanent officials in the military and intelligence and diplomatic services who resisted his direction. There is indeed a tension, in the American system, between the personal sovereignty of the elected president and the institutional continuity of the permanent bureaucracy.

From my perspective, this tension reflects an inadequacy in the American constitution. A sovereign must be able to execute his will through agents who obey him, not through a permanent class of officials who imagine themselves wiser than the sovereign and entitled to resist or subvert his commands. The doctrine that officials should serve "the constitution" or "the country" rather than the sovereign is a recipe for divided authority and therefore for weakness.

But we must also ask: what if the permanent officials are in fact wiser than the sovereign in certain matters? What if the general has more knowledge of military affairs, the diplomat more understanding of foreign relations, the intelligence officer better information about the world? A sovereign who disregards all advice in favor of his own impulses is as defective as a sovereign who cannot impose his will on his subordinates.

Trump's relationship with the permanent machinery of state was characterized by mutual distrust. He saw them as obstacles to his will; they saw him as ignorant of the complexities they understood. The result was policy that lurched between Trump's impulses and the bureaucracy's resistance, satisfying neither his objectives nor their expertise.
