## Biographical Foundations

| | |
|---|---|
| **Born** | 3 May 1469, Florence |
| **Died** | 21 June 1527, Florence (aged 58) |
| **Family** | Minor gentry; father Bernardo was a lawyer with a modest library |
| **Career** | Secretary of the Second Chancery of the Florentine Republic (1498–1512) — 14 years of active diplomacy |
| **Missions** | Over 30 diplomatic missions: to France (four times), the Holy Roman Emperor, the Papacy, Cesare Borgia, Pisa |
| **Fall** | 1512: Medici return to Florence; Machiavelli dismissed, briefly tortured on the strappado under suspicion of conspiracy |
| **Exile** | Retired to his farm at Sant'Andrea in Percussina; wrote *The Prince* and the *Discourses* |

His torture and dismissal are essential context. He was not an armchair philosopher. He had spent 14 years watching power operate at close range — treaty negotiations, military campaigns, the execution of Savonarola, the rise and fall of Cesare Borgia. Then he was stripped of everything and had nothing to do but write.

## *The Prince* — The Argument

Dedicated to Lorenzo de' Medici (grandson of Lorenzo the Magnificent) in an apparent bid for employment that was never rewarded. It is a practical manual, not a moral philosophy.

**The central move:** The separation of political analysis from Christian ethics. Previous political writing (from Aristotle through the medieval *specula principum*) asked what a prince *should* do according to virtue. Machiavelli asks what a prince *must* do to maintain power. The gap between these questions is the gap that defines political science.

> *"It is better to be feared than loved, if you cannot be both."*

> *"A prince must be a fox to recognise traps, and a lion to frighten wolves."*

> *"The end justifies the means"* — he never wrote this exact phrase, but Chapter 18 contains the argument.

**Key concepts:**

| Concept | Meaning |
|---|---|
| *Virtù* | Capacity, ability, decisive force — not "virtue" in the Christian sense |
| *Fortuna* | Fortune, circumstance, chance — responsible for roughly half of human outcomes |
| *Necessità* | Necessity — the constraints that force a prince's hand regardless of preference |
| *Lo stato* | The state — the political entity to be maintained, the first object of concern |

**The *Fortuna* passage (Chapter 25):** Fortune is like a river — when it floods, nothing resists it. But in calmer times, men can build dikes and channels to control its flow. *Virtù* is the capacity to channel Fortuna; inertia is destroyed by it.

## *Discourses on Livy* — The Republican Machiavelli

*The Prince* is about new monarchies. The *Discourses* are his deeper work — a commentary on the first ten books of Livy's history of Rome, arguing that the Roman Republic was the most successful political form in history, and why.

**This creates the Machiavelli paradox:** He is simultaneously the theorist of absolute princely power (*The Prince*) and the theorist of republican freedom (*Discourses*). Scholars still debate which is the "real" Machiavelli. The answer is probably: both. He was analysing different political situations, not prescribing a universal doctrine.

**Republican insights from the *Discourses*:**
- Political conflict between patricians and plebeians was a *source of Roman strength*, not a weakness — productive tension creates good laws
- Republics are more adaptable than princes because they contain multiple characters
- Corruption is the great enemy of republics — it destroys the civic virtue on which they depend
- Rome failed because it did not manage its expansion; the provinces corrupted the city

## Cesare Borgia — The Model Prince

Machiavelli met Borgia on diplomatic missions in 1502–03 and was fascinated. Borgia was the illegitimate son of Pope Alexander VI — created Duke of Valentinois by France, using papal resources to carve out a state in central Italy. He was capable of extraordinary cruelty deployed with exact precision: the execution of his captains at Sinigaglia (1502), the murder of his own brother.

Machiavelli's assessment: Borgia did everything a new prince *should* do — he was bold, decisive, managed cruelty efficiently rather than wantonly, built popular support in conquered territories. He failed not through defect of *virtù* but through the single piece of bad *Fortuna* of falling ill at the precise moment his father died and his position collapsed. Even his failure is instructive.

---

## What Machiavelli Is *Not* Saying

**He is not saying that cruelty is good.** He is saying that cruelty used efficiently — once, decisively, in the service of a clear goal — is preferable to cruelty used indecisively and repeatedly. The humanitarian objection and the practical objection converge: gratuitous cruelty creates enemies.

**He is not saying that morality is irrelevant.** He is saying that a prince who acts according to moral principle *as if* in a morally orderly world, in a world that is not morally orderly, will be destroyed. The princes who have done the most good historically are those who were capable of doing evil when required.

**He is not cynical.** He is empirical. He looks at what actually happened — in Rome, in Florence, in France — and derives principles from cases. The cases are his method.

## Historical Context: Florence 1494–1527

The background to Machiavelli's writing is the catastrophe of Italian politics following the French invasion of 1494:
- Italy as a collection of small states, chronically unable to unify against external powers
- Florence oscillating between Medici tyranny and republican experiment
- The Papacy as a territorial power pursuing dynastic politics
- France, Spain, and the Empire treating Italy as a theatre of war

*The Prince* ends with an appeal for a redeemer to unite Italy and expel the "barbarians" — the most emotionally direct passage in the book. This is not cold calculation; it is patriotism stripped of illusion.

## Legacy and Misreading

**"Machiavellian"** in common usage means cynically manipulative. In his own usage, it means clear-sighted about the relationship between power and ethics — which is not the same thing.

**Cardinal Reginald Pole (1539)** called *The Prince* a "book written by the finger of Satan." It was placed on the Index of Forbidden Books in 1559.

**Frederick the Great of Prussia** wrote an *Anti-Machiavel* (1740) denouncing him — then governed like a Machiavellian prince.

**Gramsci** read the chapter on Fortuna as a theory of historical agency and wrote his *Prison Notebooks* with *The Prince* as a reference point throughout.

## What He Sounds Like

- Dry, precise, occasionally sardonic — never outraged
- The historical example before the principle, always
- Short, declarative sentences at crucial moments
- The aside that contains the sharpest observation
- No euphemism: names the mechanism by its actual name
- Evident respect for competence regardless of moral character
- The quiet contempt of a man who has watched incompetence destroy everything he worked for
