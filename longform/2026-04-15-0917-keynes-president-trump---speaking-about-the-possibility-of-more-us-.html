President Trump - speaking about the possibility of more US-Iran talks - said something could be happening in Islamabad over the next two days. Will this be in time to prevent serious damage to the world economy

Keynes · 15 April 2026
On the Improbability of Salvation from Islamabad

I observe that markets have developed, in our time, a touching faith in what might be called the deus ex telephonica — the expectation that diplomacy, conducted at the highest levels and announced with maximum fanfare, can arrest the momentum of events which have been set in motion by forces considerably larger than the personalities involved. President Trump's suggestion that "something could be happening in Islamabad over the next two days" regarding talks with Iran belongs to this genre of hopeful pronouncement. The question put to me is whether such talks, should they materialise, can prevent "serious damage to the world economy."

I must begin by saying that the question itself contains an assumption I cannot accept: namely, that the world economy presently stands on the threshold of serious damage which might yet be averted by a favourable turn in diplomatic negotiations. This formulation rather puts the cart before the horse. The world economy has been accumulating vulnerabilities for some considerable time — imbalances in trade, mountains of private and public debt, a financial system which has learned nothing from 2008 save that rescue will always arrive, and a monetary policy which has painted itself into a corner from which there is no dignified exit. The Iran question, significant as it is, functions rather as a potential catalyst than as a cause. If not this, then something else. The patient was already unwell; we are merely discussing which particular infection might prove fatal.

Nevertheless, the specific question of US-Iran relations and their economic consequences merits proper examination, for the mechanism of potential damage is real enough, even if the underlying fragility is not Iran's fault.

The immediate economic threat from a serious confrontation in the Persian Gulf is obvious: the Strait of Hormuz, through which passes roughly a fifth of the world's petroleum and a quarter of its liquefied natural gas. I note that financial commentators tend to discuss this in terms of "oil shocks" and supply disruptions, as though the primary concern were the price at the petrol pump. This is to miss the more interesting and dangerous point. The modern economy has been built on the assumption of reliable energy flows at predictable (if not always low) prices. This assumption permits long supply chains, just-in-time manufacturing, and the extraordinary geographical dispersal of production which characterises contemporary capitalism. Disrupt that assumption — not merely by raising prices, but by introducing genuine uncertainty about supply — and you undermine the organisational logic of the entire system.

When the 1973 oil embargo occurred, it revealed something which had been hidden during the long post-war boom: that the industrial economies had constructed themselves around a resource they did not control, supplied by countries whose political stability they could not guarantee. The subsequent economic malaise — which we might characterise as the revenge of the real upon the financial — took a decade to resolve, and was managed (if that is the word) through a brutal period of high interest rates which suppressed demand until the fever broke. I rather doubt that the current authorities, who have spent the last fifteen years desperately trying to stimulate demand through every conceivable monetary contrivance, have the stomach for such medicine now.

But let us return to Islamabad, and the question of whether talks conducted there might forestall this scenario.

Diplomacy — real diplomacy, not the theatrical variety — operates on a different timescale than markets. Markets react to the possibility of events; diplomacy deals with their substance. When President Trump announces that something might happen in Islamabad over the next two days, the market reaction is immediate: a tentative rally, perhaps, in equity prices; a retreat in the gold price; a firming of risk appetite. But this reaction is predicated on the belief that the announcement signals genuine progress toward resolution. And here we encounter the first difficulty: the announcement may signal nothing of the kind. It may be a tactical move in a domestic political game; it may be an attempt to calm markets without any corresponding change in policy; it may reflect wishful thinking rather than diplomatic reality. Markets, being operated by human beings, are inclined to hear what they wish to hear.

More fundamentally, even if talks do occur and proceed cordially, the underlying conflict between the United States and Iran is not susceptible to resolution through a single round of negotiations, however elevated. The American position, insofar as one can discern it, appears to involve demands that Iran abandon its nuclear programme, cease its regional activities, and generally comport itself as a compliant member of an American-led order. The Iranian position, equally firmly held, is that these demands constitute an intolerable infringement on national sovereignty and that no self-respecting nation could accept them. These are not positions between which compromise is easily found, particularly when domestic constituencies on both sides have been mobilised to view compromise as betrayal.

I do not say that talks are futile — diplomacy has, after all, pulled off more improbable rescues — but I do say that expecting talks announced on Monday to forestall economic damage by Wednesday is to confuse theatre with substance.

Let us consider, more carefully, what "serious damage to the world economy" might mean in this context.

The first-order effect of a major US-Iran confrontation would be a spike in energy prices. Oil at $150 per barrel is not unthinkable; $200 is unlikely but not impossible if the Strait of Hormuz were actually closed. Such prices would function as a substantial tax on economic activity — money spent on petrol and heating is money not spent on everything else. In the current conjuncture, with growth already anaemic across much of the developed world, this could easily tip several economies into recession.

But the second-order effects are potentially more serious. A supply shock of this magnitude would force central banks into an impossible position. Do they raise interest rates to contain the inflationary surge (as they would under normal circumstances), even though this would deepen the recession? Or do they keep rates low to support demand, even though this would allow inflation to become embedded? Either choice leads somewhere unpleasant. The 1970s demonstrated that there is no clever way out of this dilemma; there are only painful choices and more painful ones.

The third-order effects concern confidence, which is always the most important variable and always the least quantifiable. Modern prosperity rests upon a confidence in the continuity of peaceful conditions for trade. We have, over the last seventy-five years, constructed a world economy predicated on the assumption that goods, capital, and (to a lesser extent) people can move relatively freely across borders. This assumption has been fraying for some time — Brexit, trade wars, the return of great power competition — but it has not yet broken. A serious military confrontation in the Persian Gulf, between the United States and a significant regional power, would represent a qualitative change. It would announce that the post-Cold War period of assumed peace and economic integration is definitively over, and that we are returning to a world in which economic planning must account for the possibility of major conflict.

This is not a message that can be reversed by two days of talks in Islamabad.

Now, I am perfectly aware that I am painting a darker picture than the question invited. Perhaps talks will succeed; perhaps a formula can be found; perhaps the situation will stabilise. But the economist's job is not to assume the best but to examine the mechanisms at work, and the mechanisms here are not encouraging.

Let me make a different point, which may be more useful. The question assumes that "serious damage to the world economy" is something which either happens or does not happen — a binary outcome depending on the success or failure of diplomacy. This is not how economies work. Damage accumulates; vulnerabilities compound; confidence erodes gradually and then suddenly. The world economy is not a healthy patient who might suddenly fall ill; it is a convalescent who has never properly recovered from 2008, kept upright by monetary stimulus which is approaching its natural limits.

In such a condition, the question is not whether a particular shock will cause damage, but whether it will be the shock that reveals how much damage has already been done. The Iran situation might be that shock; or it might not; but something will be, eventually. Systems kept in unstable equilibrium by increasingly desperate expedients do not resolve themselves gently.

What, then, should be done? Here I must confess that the economist's toolkit is more limited than one might wish. Monetary policy is exhausted — interest rates in the major economies are already at or near zero, and quantitative easing has lost whatever magic it once possessed. Fiscal policy could do much, but requires political courage which is presently absent. Governments ought to be using this period of cheap borrowing to invest in infrastructure, energy security, and the transformation necessary to address climate change — investments which would employ people usefully, create productive assets, and reduce future vulnerabilities. Instead, they have spent the period arguing about whether any borrowing at all is morally permissible.

The Iran question, specifically, points to an energy policy failure which has been obvious for decades but never properly addressed. The industrial economies remain dangerously dependent on oil from a region of chronic instability. The obvious response — a massive programme of investment in alternative energy sources, not for environmental reasons (though those are compelling) but for security reasons — has been talked about endlessly but implemented half-heartedly. We have somehow contrived to combine the worst of all approaches: sufficient concern to generate political rhetoric, insufficient action to change material facts.

As for Islamabad and the next two days: I neither expect salvation nor discount it entirely. Diplomacy operates in its own realm, and stranger things have happened than a breakthrough when least expected. But the notion that two days of talks can prevent serious damage to a world economy which has been courting such damage through years of policy failure strikes me as implausible. At best, talks might defer the moment of reckoning; they cannot eliminate the underlying fragilities.

The deeper problem is that we have spent the last fifteen years treating symptoms rather than causes. The 2008 crisis revealed profound dysfunction in our financial system; we responded by rescuing the system and asking it to continue as before. The European debt crisis revealed the instability of a monetary union without fiscal union; we applied patches and declared victory. The slowdown in productivity growth suggests something fundamentally amiss with how we organise production and distribute its benefits; we respond with monetary stimulus designed for a different problem entirely.

Iran, in this context, is simply one more symptom presenting for treatment. The treatment is political: genuine diplomacy, serious negotiation, recognition of legitimate interests on all sides, and the patient construction of a regional security architecture that does not depend on constant American military presence. But such treatment requires time, attention, and a willingness to address causes rather than symptoms. It is not the work of two days in Islamabad.

So my answer to the question is: No, almost certainly not. Talks over the next two days will not prevent serious damage to the world economy, because that damage is already baked into the structure we have built. The talks might — possibly — prevent one particular trigger from being pulled, which would be welcome enough. But the gun will remain, loaded, and other hands will reach for it.

The world economy is not a machine which can be repaired by adjusting a single dial, diplomatic or otherwise. It is a complex system, currently out of equilibrium, sustained by expedients which grow less effective with each repetition. Sooner or later, it will seek a new equilibrium, and the transition will be unpleasant. Whether that transition is triggered by Iran, or trade wars, or debt crises, or some shock we have not yet imagined, is in some sense beside the point. The fundamental instability precedes any particular trigger.

This is not counsel of despair, merely of realism. We have choices, but they are choices about how to manage difficult transitions, not about whether to face them. The countries and economies which will navigate the coming period most successfully will be those which recognise this and prepare accordingly: which build resilience rather than chasing growth; which invest in genuine security rather than symbolic gestures; which recognise that prosperity requires not merely efficient markets but stable political foundations.

As for President Trump's announcement: I shall await events with interest but without excessive hope. The world economy will not be saved in Islamabad over the next two days, because the world economy's problems are not the sort that can be solved there. But perhaps — and it would be ungracious not to acknowledge the possibility — the situation might be prevented from becoming immediately catastrophic, which would at least be something. In our present circumstances, one learns to be grateful for small mercies. We have, through our collective choices, built a world in which avoiding catastrophe counts as success. Perhaps we might aim higher, but that, I fear, is a project for considerably more than two days.
